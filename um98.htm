9.8 Channel Capacity Fraction CCF

 

If we consider a lens to be a channel that transmits information, one can ask how much information can get through.  This is the purpose of the CCF command.  The format is

 

CCF [ icol / P / M ]

 

where icol is the color number, "P" is the primary color, and "M" requests a multicolor analysis.  The default is "M".

 

The program calculates approximately how many discrete images can fit at the image plane inside the aperture of that surface.  Doing this requires some judgment, since one must determine how close adjacent images can be and still be resolved.  If we create two nearly perfect diffraction patterns and separate them by twice the Airy disk radius, they are almost resolved, as shown on the first picture below.

 

 

 

Since we judge this to be too close for reliable discrimination, we elect to space them a distance or 2.5 times the Airy disk radius, as shown on the second picture.

 

In order to evaluate the number of images that will fit in the image area, we take that area (which must be circular) and divide by an area that is 1.25 times the Airy disk in radius.  That is the number of bits of information that would get through the lens if there were no aberrations, taking a bit as a 1 or 0, corresponding to the presence or absence of a given image point.

 

In the presence of aberrations, we have to estimate the area of a single image.  To do so we calculate the diffraction MTF and look for the spatial frequency that yields an MTF value that is the same as the MTF of the perfect system at a spatial frequency that corresponds to the desired separation of images.  The inverse of that should be the separation of the aberrated image that would be resolvable, and it is greater than that calculated for a perfect image, so the number of resolved points is lower than in the perfect case.  The CCF value is just the ratio of the real-image count divided by the perfect count.

 

The calculation is performed at 19 points in the meridional plane, from 0.1 to the edge of the field.  This means that the results are accurate only if the aberrations are symmetric with field and the field is circular.  The lens should be corrected well enough that the MTF does not fall below a value of 0.58 at the first calculated point.  In other words, this feature is intended for well-corrected lenses.

 

For example, if we analyze the relay telescope, which is nearly perfect on axis but less so at the edge of the field, we get the following output:

 

SYNOPSYS AI>CCF M

 

****************************************************

        CHANNEL CAPACITY FRACTION CCF

****************************************************

MULTICOLOR ANALYSIS

POLARIZATION AND COATINGS ARE IGNORED

PARAXIAL F/NUMBER ON AXIS IN Y:            7.00683

PARAXIAL FULL-FIELD IMAGE HEIGHT,:        -12.4178

PARAXIAL CHANNEL CAPACITY:                0.391655E+07

SUM OF DISCRETE IMAGES OVER FIELD          777535.

CHANNEL CAPACITY FRACTION                 0.198525

 

SYNOPSYS AI>CCF P

 

****************************************************

        CHANNEL CAPACITY FRACTION CCF

****************************************************

ANALYSIS IN COLOR  2

POLARIZATION AND COATINGS ARE IGNORED

PARAXIAL F/NUMBER ON AXIS IN Y:            7.00683

PARAXIAL FULL-FIELD IMAGE HEIGHT,:        -12.4178

PARAXIAL CHANNEL CAPACITY:                3.916549E+06

SUM OF DISCRETE IMAGES OVER FIELD         1.687988E+06

CHANNEL CAPACITY FRACTION                 0.430989

 

This tells us that the single-color analysis (color 2) yields a fraction of 0.420989, while analyzing all colors gives a value of 0.198525, owing to the larger image size in color 3.

 

This information might be useful when comparing two systems with different aberration content.

 

The program can include the effect of polarization on the image and can analyze a pure AFOCAL system -- but not an ACCOM system.

 

The image surface should be flat or nearly so; accuracy may be reduced if it is strongly curved.
