5.39 T/number calculation (TN)                        Previous Next TOC

 

Several features of SYNOPSYS will account for energy loss from various causes.  Most of them are easily accessed via the dialog MTR, which lists the options and the restrictions.

 

1. GTRANS in Section 5.34 calculates the transmission of a single lens along the axis, and analyzes only the absorption within the glass at many wavelengths.

 

2. PTRACE considers vignetting, apodization, and coatings of the entire system – but not absorption.

 

3. TRACE considers vignetting and apodization, but not coatings or absorption.

 

4. PRAY analyzes the path of a single ray, and evaluates the loss due to coatings.

 

5. MAP can map the transmission over the field or the pupil, and considers the same effects as TRACE.

 

6.  Another feature will show the transmission curve for a selected glass type in graphic form.  This is found under the PAD GlassTable feature.

 

7. RTRANS will analyze the absorption along a given ray, ignoring other effects.

 

8.  The AI noun “TRANSMISSION” will trace a ray and account for all effects.  For this analysis you would use input such as

 

 FIND THE TRANS FOR HBAR = 1 AND YEN = .5

 

(Note that you must use at least four characters here, to distinguish “TRANSMISSION” FROM “TRACE”, which is a valid command not used in an AI sentence.)

 

9. ILLUM will find the illumination falloff over the field.  This considers all effects, including obliquity at the image.

 

Yet another feature will find the effective T/number of the system at a requested field point, incorporating all of the above effects, including vignetting, apodization, coating losses, and glass absorption – but not obliquity, which does not change the T/number.  Input is

 

 TN HBAR GBAR [ ICOL / P ]

 

HBAR and GBAR are the fractional field in (Y,X), and ICOL is the color number to be analyzed.  P specifies the primary color.

 

Here is an example.  This lens has an obscuration on the first doublet, apodization specified on surface 5, and quarter-wave AR coatings on all elements.  (This lens is also in polarization mode, since coatings would be ignored otherwise.)

 

 

The command

 

 TN 1 0

 

gives the following listing:

 

SYNOPSYS AI>TN 1 0

Rayset   Thru lens   Energy/Ray        Energy Thru   *Polarization

   577     383      0.666000E+0  0.442076E+00    0.429355E+00

THE ABOVE ANALYSIS IGNORES ABSORPTION

 

T-NUMBER ANALYSIS AT FIELD (HBAR,GBAR) =     1.0000    0.0000

COLOR NUMBER  2  0.587560 uM

EFFECT           X         Y

----------------------------------

PARAXIAL       0.0000    3.5714

GEOMETRIC      3.5295    3.2358

VIGNETTING     4.3322    3.9716

APODIZATION    5.3085    4.8666

COATINGS       4.3959    4.0300

ABSORPTION     4.3373    3.9763

TOTAL          5.3929    4.9440

 

COSINE OF INCIDENT ANGLE OF CENTER RAY AT IMAGE =   0.997804

 

The first three lines give the same information as the PTRACE command.  The number of rays through the lens has been reduced by the obscuration; the Energy/Ray is reduced by the apodization; Energy Thru is the ray fraction times the Energy/Ray; and the effects of coatings and polarization reduce the energy to the last value, 0.4294.  The only thing missing so far is the effect of absorption in the glass, as stated in the listing.

 

That is provided by the table that follows, which reduces these data to an effective T/number.  Thus the paraxial F/number is given in the first line of the table, and since this lens is not in XPXT mode, the X value is not calculated.  The geometric F/number is found in X and Y by tracing real rays at the requested field point.  The T/number is raised by energy losses from the other effects on the list.  For example, if apodization were the only cause of energy loss, the T/number would be 4.8666 in the Y-direction.  The TOTAL line gives the T/number when all causes are accounted for.

 

The effect of coatings is of course considered only if the lens is in polarization mode and coatings have been assigned.  If the polarization state is UNPOLARIZED, the loss due to surface reflections is averaged between the X and Y linear states, while the other items affecting the total are taken for Y polarization only.

 

If your goal is to estimate the flux density at the image, then you also need to factor in the obliquity of the beam with respect to the local surface normal.  The last line gives the cosine of this angle.   The lens is approximately telecentric, so in this case the falloff is not great.
