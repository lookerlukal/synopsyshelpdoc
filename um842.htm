1. Off-Axis Telescope

 

Assume the telescope shown below has been entered in an RLE file.

 

 RLE

ID TEL 8.4.2                             62002

 WAVL .6562700 .5875600 .4861300

 APS               1

 GLOBAL

 UNITS INCH

 OBB  0.000000    1.5000000    2.5000000   0.0000000000000        1.5000000    0.0000000    2.5000000

   0 AIR

   1 CV      0.0000000000000   TH      6.00000000 AIR

   2 CV      0.0000000000000   TH      6.00000000 AIR

   3 CV      0.0000000000000   TH      0.00000000 AIR

   4 RAD    -30.0000000000000   TH     -6.00000000 AIR

   4 CC     -1.00000000

   4 DECEN      0.00000000     -6.00000000      0.00000000   100

   4 AT      0.00000000      0.00000000   100

   4 REFLECTOR

   4 PTH   -2      1.00000000      0.00000000

   5 CV      0.0000000000000   TH     -6.00000000 AIR

   5 PTH   -1      1.00000000      0.00000000

   6 CV      0.0000000000000   TH     -3.00000000 AIR

   6 TH      -3.00000000

   6 YMT      0.00000000

   7 CV      0.0000000000000   TH      0.00000000 AIR

 END

 

Surfaces 4 through 7 were decentered with the command

 

4 DEC 0 -6 0 100

 

and the entry

 

OBB 0 1.5 2.5 0 1.5

 

gave the entrance aperture diameter of 5.0 inches and a field of view of 1.5 by 1.5 degrees.  We wish to place baffles at surfaces 1, 2, 5, and 6, and desire to know the required opening sizes.

 

 

The primary mirror is to be edged to a square shape, of 3 inches on a side, with the center of the opening on the original axis.

 

To input the rectangular aperture on the mirror and get a plot of the beam size at surfaces 5 and 6, we would enter

 

CHG

4 RAO 3 3 0 6             This is the square aperture, decentered upward by 6 units.

END

PUP 3 50

PLOT 5 .5

TRACE 2 1 1 150

TRACE 2 -1 1 150

TRACE 2 1 -1 150

TRACE 2 -1 -1 150

END

PLOT 6 1

TRACE 2 1 1 150

TRACE 2 -1 1 150

TRACE 2 1 -1 150

TRACE 2 -1 -1 150

END

 

This input will produce two pictures showing superimposed rim-ray figures from the four corner field points.  The plot of surface 5 is drawn at one-half scale, while a full-scale plot is requested for surface 6 because the results will fit on the 8-1/2 x 11 inch page. PLOT 1... and PLOT 2... would give the same analysis on surfaces 1 and 2, the portion of the baffles used by the entering beam.  Note that the request for 150 rays is overridden by the PUP 3 input: each rim-ray figure will contain 50 rays.  An example of what the output for surface 6 would look like is given below.

 

 

2. Entrance Pupil Map

 

The figure below shows a Cassegrain telescope with a spider support; we want a map of the entrance pupil showing the obscuration for an off-axis image.

 

 RLE

ID TEL 8.4.2A                            62002

 WAVL .6562700 .5875600 .4861300

 APS               1

 UNITS INCH

 OBB  0.000000    1.0000000    2.0000000   0.0000000000000        0.0000000    0.0000000    2.0000000

   0 AIR

   1 RAI      0.10000000       4.10000000       0.00000000       0.00000000

   1 CV      0.0000000000000   TH      0.00000000 AIR

   2 RAI      4.10000000       0.10000000       0.00000000       0.00000000

   2 CV      0.0000000000000   TH      2.00000000 AIR

   3 CAI      0.30000000       0.00000000       0.00000000

   3 RAD    -11.1607128283444   TH     -4.63999987 AIR

   3 CC     -1.00000000

   3 REFLECTOR

   4 RAD     -2.3219593116197   TH      4.95019531 AIR

   4 CC     -2.12684000

   4 REFLECTOR

   5 CV      0.0000000000000   TH      0.00000000 AIR

 END

 

 

The spider was input by placing RAI's on surfaces I and 2:

 

CHG

1 RAI .1 4.1

2 RAI 4.1 .1

3 CAl .3

END

 

Surface 3 is given a CAI to model the perforation in the primary mirror.  Here is the input required to get a footprint of the full-field pupil:

 

ON 21

PLOT 3 1.5

TRACE 2 1 0 500

END

 

The ON 21 input causes the obscured rays to appear on the plot as a numeral giving the surface number at which they stopped.  The resulting plot appears below.

 

 

This plot is a good illustration of the reason why the effects of a spider support are difficult to model with a ray-based analysis.  Although the spider is evident in this figure, showing up as a row of 2's and a column of l's, the plot could have been requested with a smaller number of rays – and if the space between rows and columns happened to fall where the spiders lay, they would not be visible and would not have any affect on the image analysis.  When modeling a spider, it is usually necessary to trace a larger number of rays than otherwise, and to examine the footprint to be sure that the obscuration is evident.
