BTOL Format Summary

 

The program BTOL is used to compute tolerances for an entire lens.  Whereas the program TOL, discussed in the previous chapter, provides a table of inverse sensitivities for user-supplied variables and aberrations, BTOL considers all of the possible variables and constructs a table of tolerances for the whole lens based on statistical considerations.  Numerous weighting and control parameters may be input to control field weights, the relative difficulty of holding parameters, to specify radii for which tooling already exists, and so on, making the program quite flexible for dealing with a variety of actual lenses and optical shops.  In addition, several adjustment parameters may be specified to help correct the defects caused by individual perturbations, and these may in some cases relax the tolerances on some variables.  Finally, a Monte-Carlo option is available to verify the statistics of the resultant error budget.

 

The menu MSB (Menu, Simple BTOL) will help you set up and run BTOL in some simple but common situations.  We recommend, however, that you read the rest of this chapter so that you will understand what is going on, even if you elect to use this simple interface.

 

BTOL utilizes the fact that for problems involving a large number of variables randomly distributed within specified ranges, the cumulative effects of these variables may be statistically predicted.  The problem then reduces to one of specifying the mean value and standard deviation of each variable, and the corresponding quantities for the overall performance may be easily calculated. Section 12.3 gives a brief summary of the theoretical considerations involved and the results that apply to lens tolerancing.  The following paragraphs describe the operation of the program and the required input.

 

BTOL will consider the change of boresight, magnification, and distortion, and either the wavefront variance, the spot variance, the Strehl ratio, or the MTF as quality descriptors.

 

The program operates in three phases: in the first the lens is dissected to identify variables subject to manufacturing error.  These include radii, conic constant, thicknesses and airspaces, Nd and Vd for glasses, surface wedge, irregularity and edge roll, and element tilts and decenters.  A set of quality descriptors to be monitored for degradation is input, along with the maximum change or tolerance limit.  Various user inputs may affect this phase.  The program does not support any of the unusual surface shapes (USS types), so if those are to be toleranced, they should be recast to a Zernike surface (with RZS) first.  Not all shapes can be successfully recast, but if the fit is satisfactory, the PFTEST tolerance will be useful in such cases.

 

In the second phase the program calculates the first and second derivatives of the quality descriptors with respect to each of the variables, and prints a table of inverse sensitivities.  These give the maximum tolerance of each variable assuming all other variables to be perfect, computed so that no quality descriptor exceeds its maximum amount.

 

The third phase is the calculation of the error budget.  This is done in an iterative fashion, by calculating the mean value and standard deviation of the quality descriptors based on the current budget, and then adjusting individual tolerances so as to bring all of the descriptors within their ranges while maintaining the tolerances as loose as possible.  There are several considerations that apply to this step.

 

There is no unique answer to the problem of budget preparation.  An infinite number of budgets may be mathematically correct, but some of them will result in lower fabrication cost than others.  The problem is to compute a practical budget, and this depends primarily on the relative difficulty of holding each variable within its limits. BTOL addresses this issue in the following way:

 

Each variable is assigned a "range", which may be controlled by optional user input, and which defines the largest tolerance that has economic consequences.  It generally costs no more to hold an element thickness to plus or minus .005 inches, for example, than to a much larger value.  The Initial budget places each variable at the end of its range.  The derivative increment is also equal to the range in effect, although the program will reduce it on an individual basis if it results in ray failures or excessive degradation of the MTF or Strehl ratio.

 

At each subsequent iteration in preparing the budget, the program finds the quality descriptor most out of tolerance, and the relative usefulness of each variable for reducing its value.  A variable is useful if it strongly influences the descriptor and if it is still comfortably within its range.  The tolerances are reduced for each variable in proportion to its relative usefulness compared to the other variables, and the procedure repeated.  At each iteration the quality descriptor controlling the process may change, depending on the effects of the previous iteration.  If a particular variable is already quite restricted compared to its range, it will be affected less and less by the process, and other variables will take up the burden.  In this way a successful budget is always produced while tending to maintain all tolerances as near the end of their ranges as possible.

 

There is no guarantee, of course, that there will not be some very tight tolerances after the budget is prepared.  This reflects the reality that for some lenses there are tight tolerances.  It is possible to relax these by a variety of means, however.

 

For example, the most obvious way is to improve the lens so that there is more allowable degradation.  You can desensitize your lens with the third-order targets listed in Section 10.13, which can sometimes be very helpful.  A third way is to fit as many curvatures as possible to existing testplates, which we may assume are measured to greater accuracy than that with which they may be controlled.  Matched surfaces may be indicated in the BTOL input, and they will be held much closer than unmatched surfaces, with the result that the other tolerances have less of the burden and can be looser.  If individual elements are fabricated and the thicknesses measured, the lens design may be recomputed and retoleranced with the specified radii and thicknesses specified as "exact" quantities – which again will result in looser tolerances for the remaining variables.  Melt data, too, may be obtained and the effects of index and dispersion variables then ignored.  If a particular variable is very hard to hold it may be assigned a larger range, and the budget recomputed.  Other tolerances will then be tighter to compensate.  If it is possible to perform adjustments at assembly, the program can determine the advantage of so doing, and will indicate the expected amount of adjustment that will be required.  Up to four adjustments may be specified to BTOL, including radii, thicknesses or airspaces, and tilts or decenters of individual surfaces or groups of surfaces.  The ATABLE function (see Section 12.1.2.2) can help you identify promising adjustment variables.  Or you may elect to run the REOPTIMIZE feature, where you may vary anything that you plan to adjust after the lenses are measured.  As a last resort, the allowed degradation may be increased.
