WorkSheet

 

The WorkSheet dialog is accessed from the SketchPAD display when you click or by typing WS in the Command Window.  This feature provides an editing window showing the input data for a selected surface or for the lens system.  To select a surface, click on the SketchPAD drawing near the vertex of the desired surface, or type a surface number (or use the spin buttons to change the number) in the number box on the Worksheet display and then click Update.  To display the system data, click the system button or enter the number 0 and then click Update.

 

When the desired data are shown, you may simply edit the numbers in the Worksheet display, and when you have finished, click Update to redraw the SketchPAD display.

WorkSheet Operations: Slider Bars

 

The worksheet also displays four slider bars with which you may change lens data in an analog fashion.  Select a surface or element as described above, and then drag a slider to the left or right to vary the assigned parameter.  The SketchPAD display is continuously updated to show the effect of your changes.  If you reach the end of the range of a bar and wish to go farther, just click on ‘Update’.  The slider will move back inside its range and you may then proceed.

 

A vertical slider is provided on the right of the dialog where you may control the sensitivity of the four horizontal sliders. Move the thumb toward the top for faster changes, and the other way for slower.

 

Curvature

Dragging this slider will change the curvature of the active surface.  Not available for certain shape options or for pickups or solves.  This slider will control the tilt angle instead of a curvature if a fold mirror is clicked, and will control the zoom position of a given zoom group if the selected surface is the start of that group and the lens is not in zoom 1.

 

Bending

Varies the curvature on both sides of an element.

 

Spacing

Varies the thickness or airspace.

 

Slide Element

Slides the element along the axis, keeping the other lenses fixed.

 

To vary a parameter not included in the above list, simply drag the mouse over the entire numeric value of that parameter in the edit pane (selecting those data and one or more blank spaces before and after) and then click on the “SEL” button.  The topmost slider will now control that parameter instead of the curvature.  To turn off this connection, click on the button again or click in the PAD display anywhere.  (The reason for selecting extra blank spaces is that when the data are altered by the slider, the number of digits displayed can change -- and the selection would otherwise not see the extra characters.)

 

If you select certain mnemonics in the edit pane, the program will prompt with the format of that entry in the tray.  Only object specifications and some surface data that requires a list of arguments are shown, since most other formats are obvious.

 

If you click on a fold mirror that is followed immediately by a dummy surface with the same tilt angle as the fold mirror, the topmost slider will control the tilt angle of both surfaces.  With this slider you can then vary the fold mirror angle, and the dummy surface angle will automatically get the same angle so the optical axis remains aligned with the reflected rays.

 

When you insert a fold mirror via the toolbar button, the program assigns a PAS (pickup asymmetries) to the second of the two surfaces involved.  The WorkSheet slider will alter both angles in any case, but if you want to vary the tilt angle of the mirror in some way outside of the WorkSheet, it is important that this pickup be assigned.

 

The bottom slider is somewhat special; normally you want to slide an element only between its neighbors, and the ends of the slider correspond to those limits.  But if you want to slide an element that is not between its neighbors, such as an element with dummies on either side some distance away, you have to offset the range of the slider.  To do so, click the checkbox at the bottom labelled “Offset”, and then move the slider around until the present value of the airspace to the left of the element is contained within the range of values at either end of the slider.  Then click the checkbox again (to turn it off) and adjust the element with the slider as usual.

 

Worksheet Operations: the Lens Layout Tool:

 

The tools described above allow you to edit the data describing the lens.  The Layout Tool lets you make changes to the paths of rays directly, and the program calculates lens data that yield that path.  This tool is intended to help you lay out a preliminary configuration, much as you might draw a sketch on paper, but it does not optimize the lens.  When you get a layout you think has potential, you have to construct an optimization MACro and begin improving the result as you normally would.

 

For example, we load the triplet starting point, open the WorkSheet, and click the LLT button.  The drawing on the top panel of the PAD display now shows the lens with some rays: the axial marginal ray shown in red, and the full-field chief-ray, in blue.  The full-field upper and lower rim rays are also shown, in grey:

 

 

Another window opens with a summary of the instructions for using this tool.

 

First, we make a checkpoint.  Then we will increase the thickness of element 1 by clicking and dragging on the handle at the blue ray on side 2 of that element.

 

 

Now we will drag the handle on the red ray at surface 6 up and to the right.  The program will find the new spacing from surface 5 and the curvature on surface 4 that yield that exact point.  (It will not vary the radius on a flat surface in this operation, but looks to the left to find a radius it can vary.)

 

 

Now we increase the thickness of element 3, again by dragging on the handle at the blue ray on surface 7.

 

 

There are other things we can do too.  If we <shift>click on the red-ray handle at surface 3 and drag right or left, we change the bending of that element:

 

 

If we <ctrl>click on that handle and drag right or left, we change the power of that element.

 

 

Notice how this change also alters element 3.  This lens has UMC and YMT solves on surface 7, which are honored continuously.  Be aware of this kind of connection, and delete any pickups or solves you don't want active before using this tool.  (The point of this, in case you are confused, is not that those are important changes for this lens.  We are only showing how the tool works.)

 

There are more changes you can make:  If you drag the red-ray button at surface 1 up or down, you change the paraxial value of YMP1; and dragging the blue-ray button on surface 1 will alter YPP1.  (Neither of those moves will work if the value of the parameter is constrained, however; the stop must be on surface 1 (to implement an implied pupil position) to use the latter, and the former will not work if the lens has the FNO attribute set.)  In other words, the parameter you want to adjust must not be constrained by anything else.

 

This tool can only alter spherical or conic surfaces, and the PAD display must show a D drawing in the top panel.  If the lens has fold mirrors so that the optical axis is not horizontal, the tool still works -- but dragging must still be in the usual direction, regardless of the ray direction.

 

To turn this tool off, click on almost any of the other buttons on the WorkSheet toolbar or click the Close button on the prompt dialog.

 

You can change the size of the edit handles in the lens layout tool with the command

 

LLT pixels

 

where pixels is the desired size.  The default size is 60 pixels.  If the PAD display is currently open, you have to close and open it again to update this value.  Thus, to double the size of the handles, you would enter

 

LLT 120

 

and then close and reopen PAD.
