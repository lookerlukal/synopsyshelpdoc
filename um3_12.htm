3.12 Hyperhemispheric surfaces

 

 

Special considerations apply to surfaces whose curves extend beyond the hemisphere point.  Many of the usual rules do not apply to such shapes, and they may require special input.

 

Here is an example of a lens with this kind of surface:

 

 

In this lens, surface 7 extends past the hemisphere point.

 

There are two situations to consider:  The first is when this kind of shape occurs naturally during lens optimization.  The surface in question remains formally a normal spherical surface, but when the program calculates the default clear apertures, it senses that the ray intercept is past that point and activates a flag whose purpose is to alert the lens drawing programs, including the PAD display, to draw the shape properly, past that point.  Vignetting is not tested in that case, and only simple edges are drawn. EFILE edges may not be assigned to such elements, since the normal edge geometry makes no sense in that case.  Only spherical surfaces are so treated, and there must be no decentered or non-circular apertures..

 

The other situation is when a design is complete and you want to assign hard clear apertures yourself.  In that case, you also must declare the surface a HYPERHEMISPHERE, since the default CAO calculation is bypassed then and the flag is not set automatically, as in the previous situation.

 

Element drawings with ELD also show the simple edge, and if you want to design the edges differently, you should declare the elements NOEDGE first.  Then ELD will not draw the edges, and you can draw the shape you want with the annotation editor.

 

The example above shows the hyperhemisphere on side 1.  It can also occur on side 2, as shown below.

 

 

This kind of surface is much more difficult to manufacture than a normal concave surface, and is to be avoided unless you have no alternative.

 

All of the lens drawing programs honor this kind of shape, except for SOLID.  But the rotating solid program RSOLID works and does much the same thing.

 

Avoiding hyperhemispherical surfaces

 

The above comments regarding the difficulty inherent in these shapes apply even more if you want a surface to be aspheric and the optimization program bends it into a hyperhemispheric shape.  Small plastic aspheres are commonly used in cell-phone camera lenses, and they can be produced at low cost by a molding process.  But it is impossible to separate a lens from the mold if it goes past the hemisphere point, and the feature that tests for that condition only applies to pure spherical shapes anyway, not to aspheres.  So one must control the shape so that situation does not occur.

 

The ray aberration DSLOPE can in some cases keep the surface slope in the region short of the hemisphere point, but that feature calculates the sags at two close points and takes the derivative with respect to Y.  The problem is that a hyperhemispheric surface has two solutions to the sag equation, and the program takes the one closest to the vertex.  For this reason, if the shape should happen to become strongly hyper at any iteration, the sag calculation will not see it and the shape cannot thereafter leave that solution.

 

Instead, in this case the ray aberration RSLOPE is recommended.  That calculates the slope relative to the optical axis, rather than to the vertex plane (as DSLOPE does), and it uses real ray intercepts instead of a simple sag calculation.

 

Thus, in a case where DSEARCH comes back with a hyper surface, as shown below where surface 2 is aspheric and extends too far,

 

 

 

one might add to the SPECIAL AANT section the entry

 

LLL 5 1 1 A P RSLOPE 1 0 1 0 2

 

This will keep the slope angle larger than 5 degrees at the full-field upper rim ray position on that surface.  In this instance, the pupil is inverted, but the program senses that and returns a positive slope for intercepts short of the hyper point.  So this operand will work regardless of the pupil orientation.
