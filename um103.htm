10.3 Aberration Input (AANT)                                        Next Previous TOC

 

Every quantity that you wish to incorporate into the merit function for optimization purposes must be described in an aberration input file, which must begin with the input

 

AANT [ P ]

 

and end with the input

 

END

 

You may create up to 25,000 aberrations, which are defined by subsequent lines, of six types:

 

1.

Ray-based aberrations, including transverse coordinates and OPD's

 

2.

Paraxial aberrations, including 3rd and 5th-order defects

 

3.

Construction parameter aberrations

 

4.

Diffraction MTF aberrations

 

5.

Composite aberrations defined by an equation using one or more of the other definitions. CLINK aberrations are similar, but involve processing a SYNOPSYS command and selecting one or more output data items.

 

6.

A constant number can also be included with the mnemonic CONST.  For example,

 

M 0 1 A P YA .5

MUL CONST 2

S GIHT

 

This will find the Y-coordinate at half field, multiply by 2, and then subtract the Gaussian image height, controlling distortion at that field point.

 

In the default mode (switch 29 on) SYNOPSYS ignores the optional [ P ] on the AANT command and automatically gives a listing of the AANT input with the output.  If switch 29 is turned off (see 10.5), the form of listing can be controlled; the [ P ] produces an input image, while omission of the P results in a more lengthy but complete summary of the aberration definitions.

 

The values of all defined aberrations will be listed at the start and at the end of every optimization run if switch 89 is turned ON.  Since the list may be lengthy, and one seldom needs to examine all of it, the default has this switch OFF.

 

All aberration definitions must be placed between the AANT and END lines. Two kinds of aberration definitions are used most frequently.  These specify a quantity to be controlled explicitly or imply a collection of such quantities.  The first uses input such as

 

.

 

Here, the GOALS section says to Miminize to a target value of 55.2 with a relative weight of 1 the quantity in the DETAILS section, in this case the TOTL length of the lens. The “A” in that section means Add this quantity.  You may have several items in the DETAILS section, combined with A, S, MUL, and DIV.  For example, to control the sum of thicknesses 4 and 5, you could enter

 

 M 34.567 A TH 4

 A TH 5.

 

The input that defines a collection of quantities to be controlled takes a form such as

 

 GNR 0.5 10 4 P 1

 

which in this example says to generate a grid of rays, weighted with parameters 0.5 and 10, 4 rays per half-pupil, color P (the primary color) at full field (HBAR = 1).  This format is explained in Section 10.3.1.1.

 

Input for the GOALS section formally takes the form

 

 .

 

This input is followed by additional parameters that describe what is being minimized (the DETAILS section).  These are in four categories, and you may read about them by following the links at the end of this section.

 

The aname is an optional string of up to 8 characters, consisting of all numbers or starting with a letter with no punctuation marks or spaces within the name.  This name will appear on the ALIST and FINAL output to help you identify individual aberrations.

 

M means to minimize to the specified target; MF means to minimize with the fourth power of the aberration, rather than the second, in the merit function. L, LIM, LL, LUL and LLL specify a one-sided limit, described in Section 10.3.5.1. C specifies exact correction based on Lagrange multipliers, described in Section 10.3.5.2.

 

SR and SCR are described in the ray aberration discussion.  Up to 5001 aberrations M's, etc.) may be defined.  Ray aberrations each may contain up to 9 components (see 10.3.1.2), and other types of aberrations any number.

 

Many of the aberration definitions allow one to refer to a particular surface number, for example, the input

 

 M 55 .1 A TH 6

 

specifies a target value of 55 for thickness number 6.

 

It is sometimes convenient, when referring to a surface number within a lens, to use the special codes

 

 LB0

 LB1

 LB2

 LB3

 LB4

 ...

 LB9

 

 FLAG

 

 FP1

 FP2

 FP3

 FP4

 ...        

 FP9

 

 FM1

 FM2

 FM3

 FM4

 ...

 FM9

 APS.

 

The first of these specify “last but zero”, “last but one”, “last but two”, and so on; these let you count backwards from the last surface.  If the lens has eight surfaces, the example referring to surface 6 could be written

 

M 55 .1 A TH LB2.

 

You may also use the mnemonics FLAG and APS to refer indirectly to a surface number.  If a surface has been declared a FLAG surface, that surface number will be substituted for the FLAG entry.  The entry FP1 means "flag plus one" and so on, letting you specify up to nine surfaces before and after the flag surface.  The surface declared the stop (with SN APS ... in the RLE file) can be referenced with the APS entry.  These are all useful when running SPBUILD or AEI, where the number of surfaces is constantly changing and referring to a particular surface by number is not practical.  These codes may be used almost anywhere in the program to refer to a surface number indirectly.  They cannot be used within an RLE or CHG file.  Also, APS cannot be used in an FFBUILD file, since that is a valid mnemonic in that case.

 

When designing zoom lenses, you can declare which zooms are to be corrected with a

 

 ZOOM nb

 

directive in the AANT file.  All aberrations defined thereafter will apply to the requested zoom until another ZOOM directive is encountered.  In the event you want the same aberrations to apply to more than a single zoom, you can use the ZGROUP ... END format.  For example,

 

ZGROUP ALL

(aberrations, raysets, etc.)

END  (to end the group)

 

or

 

ZGROUP 1 3 5 7

...

END

 

ZGROUP 2 4 6 8

...

END

 

(other AANT entries)

 

END (to end the AANT file)

 

The first example will apply the entered aberrations to all zoom groups; the second and third will apply only to the zooms entered explicitly.

 

Note that the ZGROUP entry requires an END entry (to terminate the group), and the AANT file requires a second END to terminate all aberration definitions.  A ZGROUP may not include any ZOOM or another ZGROUP declaration.

 

The entry ZFOCUS TH0 JSN DT [ GIHT ] will alter the object distance to the value TH0, increase the airspace following surface JSN by DT, and subsequent rays and aberrations will be calculated for those settings.  To restore the nominal parameters, enter ZFOCUS OFF.  The optional GIHT parameter is explained in Section 10.7.1.

 

For multiconfiguration optimization, you must enter

 

ACON nb

 

before the aberrations that are to apply to configuration nb.

 

There are, in addition to the definitions described above, several entries that are used to monitor certain aspects of the lens to keep it from becoming unreasonable.  These are

 

AEC        to monitor edge thicknesses, where TH is varying.

 

AGE        to monitor edge thickness of glass elements, where TH is varying.

 

AFE        monitors edges, as does AGE, but at the apertures given by EFILE points A and E if defined, or at the CAO if not.

 

AAE        to monitor edge thicknesses of airspaces, where TH is varying.

 

ACC        to control maximum center thicknesses of elements, where TH is varying.

 

ACM        to control minimum center thicknesses, where TH is varying.

 

ASC        to prevent surface slopes from becoming too steep at the rim rays, where CV is varying.

 

ACS        to prevent surface slopes from becoming too steep at the CAO, where CV is varying.

 

ACA        to prevent rays from entering or leaving an element too close to the critical angle, where the CV is varying.

 

ATC        checks the angle from normal of all rays traced by ray errors in the merit function.  This is to prevent critical angle errors if the angle gets too steep.

 

AAC        aperture control, to monitor clear apertures and keep them from getting too large.  This applies to all apertures not assigned a hard CAO.

 

ALA        to monitor apertures on lens surfaces.  This is similar to AAC, but does not apply to reflective surfaces.

 

AZA        to monitor the airspace on both sides, and the edge dimensions, of each zoom group in a ZFILE zoom lens.

 

ADT        to monitor the maximum ratio of lens diameter to thickness.

 

ADM        to monitor the minimum ratio of lens diameter to thickness.

 

ADS        to monitor the ratio of lens diameter to thickness, adding surface sag to the thickness.  This accounts for the greater stiffness of meniscus elements..

 

AMS        to monitor the separation between centers of curvature of meniscus lenses.

 

ARC         to monitor the position of the chief ray within the beam throughout the lens.

 

 

Each of these has optional parameters to control how they are applied.  Click the above links for a discussion of each.  Special considerations apply to ZFILE zoom lenses; see Section 10.7.3.
