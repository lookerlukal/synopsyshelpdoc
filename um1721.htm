17.2.1 Standard coatings                                                        Next Previous TOC

 

The coatings in the previous section are defined as follows:

 

NONE

specifies an uncoated surface.

 

QMD

is a quarter-wave coating of MgF2, the thickness measured at the helium d line (.58756 μM).

 

QMH

is a quarter-wave coating of MgF2, measured at the HeNe line (.6328 μM).

 

HEA

is a three-layer antireflection coating, consisting of the stack below (see section 16.1).

air

.25 1.384

MgF2

.50 2.1

ZrO2

.25 1.62

0 IND

substrate index

 

 

The thicknesses in this stack are at .58756 μM.

 

AL

is a layer of uncoated aluminum.  For this layer, we take the optical constants N = 0.867, K = 6.42.  See section 17.2.5.  This is used for reflective surfaces only.

 

ALSIO

is aluminum overcoated with a half-wave (at .58756 μM) layer of SiO at N = 1.7.  This is a reflective coating.

 

AU

is a coating of gold, where the optical constants are taken to be N = 8.843, K = 64.13.  The measured data for gold are imprecise; these values are averages from several sources and apply at a wavelength of 10 μM. (See 17.2.5.)  The coating is reflective.

 

AGSIO

is overcoated silver.  The constants for silver at a wavelength of 0.55 μM are N = 0.055, K = 3.32.  The overcoating is a half-wave layer of SiO at N = 1.7. This is a reflective coating.

 

LOSSY

is a dummy coating, provided for cases where the details of the multilayer stack are unknown.  It gives a fixed loss of 0.5% at each surface, and no phase shift.  It may be either transmissive or reflective.

 

LO2

this is also a dummy coating, this one with a fixed loss of 0.2% at each surface.

 

PERF

is a dummy coating giving no loss at the surface.  It may be either transmisive or reflective.

 

COSINE

This coating is intended to model the loss of efficiency of a solar cell with increasing angle of obliquity.  Typically, the response of photovoltaic cells drops off as the cosine of the incidence angle of the light.  Thus, light arriving at 45 degrees from the surface normal will produce only 0.707 times as much energy as normal-incidence light.

 

An example of the use of this coating is given in Section 17.4.

 

EN1

This is an enhanced coating for mirrors.  The substrate is aluminum, and there are 10 layers added.  Reflection is above 98% over the range from 0.45 um to 0.65 um.  See the notes below for more information about this coating.

 

EN2

Coating EN2 is a lower-cost enhanced coating for aluminum mirrors.  The stack has but four layers, but reflectance is above 98% over most of the visible range.

 

5050

This is for modeling beamsplitters; half of the energy is lost, but there is no change in polarization state or phase, which makes it less accurate than a real coating, which should be used if you have data for the coating stack.

                                                                 

We have endeavored to get data on commercial multilayer coatings from the optics industry, but often those data are proprietary.  Still, we want to give our users a way to analyze their system with a coating that closely matches what the industry can provide.  The coating designated EN1 is a stack we have designed in SYNOPSYS with performance that is probably representative of the best that can be obtained.  Coating EN2 was sent by a coating house.  The reflection of these coatings varies with wavelength as shown below.
