5.5 GHOST                                        Next Previous TOC

 

Several features are available for analyzing ghost images, summarized below.

 

[PU]GHOST

paraxial analysis of all two-bounce ghosts at the image, described in this section

 

RGHOST

real-ray analysis of a single two-bounce ghost

 

PGHOST

aberration definition to control a selected two-bounce ghost

 

GHPLOT

Plotted analysis of single or multiple ghost image.

 

 

BGI

paraxial analysis command listing all one-bounce (buried) ghost images

 

RGI

real-ray analysis of a single buried ghost

 

BGI

aberration definition mnemonic to control a selected buried ghost

 

FGI

power density at a single buried ghost

 

The GHOST command and its variant PUGHOST, described here, analyzes ghosts at the image surface arising from two reflections of the incoming light from lens surfaces.  Ghosts formed from a single reflection are important in high-power laser systems, and can be analyzed with the BGI, RGI, and FGI commands, described in Section 5.13.  The analysis routines may be run from the dialog MGH.

 

GHOST analyzes the paraxial value of the quantity YA, the paraxial marginal ray, while PUGHOST considers ray YB, the chief ray.  A small value of YA indicates that a reflected image of a point in object space is focused more or less sharply at the image, forming an undesirable ghost image.  A small value of YB, on the other hand, indicates that a ghost image of the entrance pupil is focused near the image, which also may be undesirable under some conditions.  Input for these two variations is otherwise the same.  An example of a system with a pupil ghost is given below.

 

When optics must operate with a bright source within the field of view, unwanted reflections off of the lens surfaces can flood the focal plane with so-called veiling glare.  Ray paths that involve two reflections off of lens surfaces are the chief source of such glare, since each possible combination of two surfaces can give rise to a ghost image that is more or less out of focus at the focal plane.  The superposition of all such ghosts constitutes the veiling glare.  The discussion below describes the GHOST command; see Section 5.5.1 for a description of an option that gives detailed information about a single ghost image.

 

The input required to analyze the ghost-image-forming properties of a lens system is as follows:

 

[PU]GHOST [ R DTS ] [ P ]

[ R REFL [ ALL ]

SN SN SN …

[ R REFL ]

SN SN SN

…

END

Note: [ R DTS ] must be entered if [ R REFL ] is.

 

The fractional reflectance of each surface is entered with the R REFL input, followed by a list of the surfaces that have the entered reflectance.  The default reflectance is 0.01.  Dummy surfaces (no index change) and mirror surfaces do not contribute to a ghost image, and are not included unless a reflectance is specifically entered.

 

If R REFL ALL is input, the given reflectance will apply to all surfaces except dummy surfaces, and no surface numbers should be entered for that instance.  This may be followed by additional R REFL … as necessary,

 

Output consists of a table of all ghosts, indicating which surfaces are responsible, the paraxial blur size, marginal ray angle at the image, and intensity.  The latter is normalized to be unity (for reflectance of 100%) if the ghost blur is the same size as DTS.  An in-focus image (i.e. much smaller than DTS) will therefore have a very large intensity.  Transmission losses due to the reflection coefficients are ignored.  A cumulative ghost table is also given where the ghosts are listed in order from the weakest to the strongest, making it simple to identify the most important ghost sources.  The default value of DTS is 1.0

 

A plot showing the accumulated ghost energy is produced if the optional P is entered.

Since a paraxial ghost image can geometrically be focused to a dimension that is smaller than the diffraction limit, a method had been provided to add a correction in such cases.  If switch 49 is turned on before the GHOST analysis is run, then the radius of each ghost is increased by an amount equal to the product of the primary wavelength times the local F/number of the ghost beam.  A ghost that is perfectly in focus will therefore appear to be about one Airy disk in size in the GHOST listing, while a larger ghost is relatively unaffected by this correction.  (This increase is not implemented for the PUGHOST variety.)

 

While it is not obvious that a pupil ghost creates any ill effects, it too can produce an unwanted ghost image.  Consider the system shown below:

 

 

In this example, light reflects off of the final surface, is reflected again at the inner surface of the leftmost lens, and then goes to the image.  The beams from the four field points shown all hit the detector in about the same place, creating a disk of light that should not be there.  This is often a consideration with astronomical optics, where the field is very dark but there may be isolated bright points of light.  In such cases, it is wise to evaluate the design with PUGHOST, and then to target any offending ghosts in the merit function, if necessary, with the PUGHOST aberration.

 

Being a paraxial calculation, the GHOST command ignores apertures, tilts, and decenters.  To evaluate a ghost with real rays, see the RGHOST command.

 

You can also control the blur radius from a selected ghost with the mnemonic PGHOST, in the AANT file.  This feature can also give detailed information about a selected ghost image, as described in the next section.
