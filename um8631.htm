8.6.3.1 Convolution MTF options (MTF, ZMTF, MFF, MZMTF etc.) Next Previous TOC

 

There are several kinds of MTF analysis available in SYNOPSYS.  Briefly, these are:

 

1.  Convolution MTF, exact OPD (MTF), described in Section 8.6.3.2

2.  Convolution MTF, interpolate OPD (ZMTF) (see above link)

 

3.  Multifield convolution MTF, exact OPD (MFF), described in Section 8.6.3.3

4.  Multifield convolution MTF, interpolate OPD (MZMTF) (see above link)

 

5.  Convolution MTF over field (MOF) , described in Section 8.6.3.6

6.  Interpolation version of MOF (ZMOF) (see above link)

7.  Through-focus convolution MTF, (TFMTF), described in Section 8.6.3.5

 

8.  Fourier-transform MTF, (DMTF), described in Section 8.6.3.4

 

9.  Geometric MTF, described in Section 8.5.3

10.  Through-focus geometric MTF (TFG) , described in Section 8.5,4

11.  Multifield geometric MTF (MGMTF) , described in Section 8.5.10

 

These options, or links to them, are found in the dialog MOP.

 

The first of these features (MTF) evaluates the MTF by sampling the pupil at a grid frequency equal to, or a multiple of, the pupil shear corresponding to the spatial frequency under analysis.  This method is fast and accurate, but it will not handle the case where the desired spatial frequency is lower than what is implied by the base shear.  The second family (ZMTF) interpolates the pupil wavefront aberration with a piecewise linear spline, and uses those data to approximate the OPD at each shear sample point.  This technique works even at low frequencies, but is slower.  In one of our tests, the MOF feature ran in about 4 seconds, while the linear fit required 54 seconds.  The difference is smaller for the MTF and ZMTF cases.  If you need an accurate analysis of the MTF at frequencies much lower than the diffraction cutoff, which is often the case when aberrations are large, then the interpolated forms of the analysis are recommend.  Although this is the slowest of the options, it is more reliable at low spatial frequencies.

 

If you run your MTF analysis and see an error message that the frequency involved was too low, switch to the interpolation method.  (The dialog MOP lets you select from these options with a mouse click.)  This protocol also applies to the MTF calculations in BTOL and for MTF aberrations.

 

As a rule, if you request a frequency less than about 1/20 of the diffraction cutoff, use the interpolation method.  If you request an MTF curve and specify a cutoff frequency that is less than about 1/3 of the diffraction cutoff at the F/number of your lens, you should also request the interpolation method.  If you do not, and the program detects the low frequency error, it will print an error message, increase the frequency to an acceptable value, and suggest the interpolation option instead.

 

In the event that the exit pupil of the lens is strongly non-circular, a given spatial frequency at the MTF requires a different shear at the entrance pupil in X and Y.  The program calculates these shears, but in extreme cases, the shear can be completely outside the entrance pupil in one direction or the other.  In this case, there are not enough data points to yield a meaningful MTF, and the program will print a message advising you to lower the cutoff frequency, which reduces the shears proportionally to the fraction of the default frequency requested.

 

The above features may be run interactively from the dialog MOP, or its links.

 

Although the convolution methods ignore polarization, they do take into account vignetting and apodization.  (Only the DMTF version considers polarization.)

 

The MTF calculated by the MTF optimization feature and by the MTF tolerancing program BTOL uses the interpolation method, and automatically selects either the exact-ray or interpolation option, depending on the spatial frequency.  The optimization MTF, however, does not treat vignetting.  If a vignetted pupil is to be considered, it is necessary to approximate the pupil with the VSET parameters during optimization (or select one of the pupil options that automatically include the effects of vignetting, such as WAP 2 or 3, or the VFIELD option.

 

Both of these families can give the MTF at a single frequency or optionally plot the MTF at 21 frequencies.  See also the forms that can plot the MTF at up to four field points on the same picture, the MTF at a single frequency at seven focus positions, or over the field, described in the sections that follow.

 

Most of the MTF family of plots can show faint grid lines in addition to the annotated X and Y axes.  To get the lines, turn on switch 87 before requesting the analysis.  The MOP dialog lets you select this option with a check box.

 

Be aware that all of the convolution MTF calculations assume that the ray grid in the exit pupil is geometrically perfect and rectangular.  If this is not the case, the shear calculation might go to the wrong point and the MTF may not be accurate.  This is easy to check: just ask for a footprint on the last surface and look for distortion of any kind.  When it doubt, use the Fourier-transform MTF calculation DMTF, which does not rely on shearing the pupil.  This is not an issue for most systems, which are usually well corrected, but we advise the user to keep this qualification in mind.
