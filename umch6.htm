Chapter 6

 

6.0 LENS ANALYSIS ROUTINES

 

 

6.1 Paraxial Raytrace

 

Whenever a lens is read in or altered in any way, a paraxial raytrace is automatically performed to satisfy the pupil solve, FILLSTOP, FFIELD, and CSTOP options, if any, and any surface pickups or solves that are present.  It also calculates the first-order quantities that appear on the SPEC output, the reverse tilt and decenters required by UNDO surfaces, and finds global coordinates if the system is in GLOBAL mode.

 

The normal output from PXT is a single line giving the Gaussian image height, focal length (for infinite object), back focal distance, vertex-to-vertex distance, and paraxial defocus. The paraxial raytrace may be run interactively from the dialog MFT, or called by the user with the input

 

PXT [ P / JSSS JSPS ]

 

The optional [ P ] causes a surface-by-surface listing of the paraxial rays, and entering two surface numbers gives output for only that portion of the lens.  Here is an example of the surface-by-surface listing:

Incident angle of marginal ray

Height of principal ray

Angle of principal ray after refraction or reflection

Ichief

Incident angle of principal ray

 

 

 

GIHT

the value of YB on the last surface (which need not be at the paraxial focus).  In AFOCAL mode, UB is output (the tangent of the paraxial chief-ray angle).

 

FOCL

the quantity ( -YMP1 ) / ( UA*( FINAL INDEX ) ) where the angle UA is measured in image space.  This calculation is done for an object at infinity, even if the actual object distance is finite.

 

BACK

the separation between the last two surfaces.  If the lens is AFOCAL, this quantity is the thickness or airspace up to the final two dummy surfaces.  In that case, if the dummies are located at the exit pupil, BACK is then the eye relief of the design.

 

TOTL

the sum of all thicknesses except BACK.  (If the system has but two surfaces, TOTL = BACK.)

 

DELF

the paraxial defocus for the actual object distance at the final surface.  A positive value indicates that the paraxial focus is to the right of the final surface.  In AFOCAL mode, the paraxial marginal ray angle UA is output.

 

FNUM

this quantity is calculated by PXT and printed by SPEC. It is defined as

-0.5 / UA at the last surface.  Note that the angle UA is computed in the final medium, which is not necessarily air.  For AFOCAL systems, the diameter of the collimated output beam is printed instead of the FNUM.

 

 

PXT ignores all asymmetries (tilts and decenters, deformations) when calculating the paraxial ray paths.  For biconic and toric surfaces, PXT uses the curvature in the Y-Z plane. Splines are treated by taking the curvature implied by the first non-zero zone.

 

For HOEs the program takes the HOE points P1 and P2 as conjugate positions and derives an effective focal length, taking the path length from the vertex of the HOE to each point as the distance.

 

The thickness before a surface defined as either GLOBAL or LOCAL is derived according to the rules in section 3.8.2, for paraxial programs, in accordance with the system flags GTS and GTZ.

 

If the lens contains any X-Z based paraxial solves or the system parameter XPXT, the PXT analysis will be performed in both the X-Z and Y-Z planes.

 

An additional paraxial quantity is printed by the SPEC command, identified as PARAXIAL FOCAL POINT.  This is the distance from the last lens surface to the paraxial focus – which may or may not be the same as the actual distance from that surface to the final surface.

 

All of these paraxial quantities can be controlled by entries in the merit function.  See Section 10.3.2.
