3.3.2.2.6 Zernike Polynomial Surfaces                Previous Next TOC

 

(These options go into an RLE or CHG file, and may be entered interactively with the dialog SPS.)

 

A surface may be defined a ZERNIKE surface with the input

 

SN ZERNIKE [ AP [ XOS YOS [ YSCALE ] ] ]

 

ZERNIKE NB COEFF

 

ZERNIKE NB COEFF

 

…

 

where up to 37 coefficients are entered on a succession of input lines.  Each NB gives the Zernike polynomial number, and COEFF is the value of that coefficient.  The ZERNIKE polynomials are orthogonal over the unit radius circle.

 

The size of the unit circle can be scaled to an aperture of radius AP (default is AP = 1).  The program does this by scaling the radial coordinate as R**2 = (X**2 + Y**2) / AP**2.  A typical use of this option is to get the coefficients from an interferogram (see ZCOE) and then to model the surface on, say, a perfect paraboloid so as to simulate the measured wavefront for evaluation or correction.  This aperture can also be declared a variable with term G 50.

 

The surface defined by the Zernike terms is normally centered at the vertex of the surface.  But it is possible to decenter the polynomial; to do so, enter the desired coordinates of the center point in XOS and YOS.  These data may also be declared variables or targets in optimization with the mnemonics G 38 and G 39.

 

The zones are normally calculated with unity scale in both X and Y, making zones with no azimuth term circular.  But you can also input an optional scale factor in Y in word 6 of the declaration.  Then the value of Y at any point on the surface is divided by YSCALE before the polynomial is calculated.  This term can be varied with G 51.

 

The theory of the Zernike expansion requires a constant term as well as the polynomials.  Some of the other terms contribute a Z-component even at the axis, and the shape that results does not necessarily pass through the vertex point, as one might expect.  Term G 37 applies a constant to the expansion and can be used to move the actual center point to the vertex plane.  It can be varied by including that G term in the variable list, and controlled by targeting the Z coordinate of the surface at the center.  It can also be controlled by the declaration

 

 sn ZVZ

 

in the RLE or CHG file.  The program will then automatically adjust term G 37 so the surface has a sag of zero at the coordinates (0,0).

 

To remove this declaration and let term G 37 be free to vary, use

 

 sn ZVF.

 

Zernike terms may be applied to a surface that also has a spherical curvature and conic constant.  The surface sag is the sum of the contributions of the latter plus the polynomials.

 

One frequently needs to add only terms with bilateral symmetry.  For convenience, those terms are listed here:

 

2 3 4 7 8 10 11 14 15 16 19 20 23 24 26 27 30 31 34 35 36 37.

 

The terms with rotational symmetry are as follows:

 

3 8 15 24 35 36 37.

 

The SPS dialog has a subdialog that lists the current terms alongside the equation of each one.  You can also access that dialog from the WorkSheet by selecting the desired surface and then clicking the Curvature Dialog button .

To vary any of the coefficients (in the PANT file) enter

 

VY SN G NB

 

where NB is the coefficient number to be varied.  Note that the surface must be defined as a Zernike surface before the PANT file is read, since the meaning of the G-variables depends on the current shape.
