5.30 DOE Fringe Pattern DMASK                        Previous NextTOC

 

The command

 

DMASK ISN MODE  [ GRID [ CAO [ SIGN ]  [ X ] ] ] ]

 

Will create a picture showing the fringe pattern associated with the polynomial terms assigned to a DOE or some kinds of USS shapes  This pattern can be used as a mask to create the DOE with photolithographic methods.

 

ISN

The surface number, which must be a DOE

 

MODE

The type of fringe pattern to show.  See below.

 

GRID

Resolution of fringe pattern.  This is limited primarily by available memory and your patience.  For adequate quality, 1000 or more may be required.  See comments, below.  The default is 1000.

 

The ZONE option uses 10 000 points, if the OPD function is axially symmetric.

 

CAO

The clear aperture to show.  Default is the current aperture assigned to the substrate of the DOE (i.e., the next surface).  Enter zero for the default.  For a DOE of type USS 16, 20, and 25 it is the CAO of the surface itself.

 

SIGN

This controls the sign of the fringes; +1 is normal, while –1 reverses the sign of the OPD function.

 

 

The available MODEs are as follows:

 

GRAY

LEVEL

B0

B1

B2

B3

ZONES

[X/Y]PROFILE

OPD

 

A DOE is a diffractive optical element, modeled as a HOE but with simpler input, or as shape USS 16, 20, 25, 26, or 27.  It resembles a Fresnel lens in that the desired curve is assigned to a series of small wedge-shaped grooves, but differs from the latter because the groove height is on the order of a wavelength of light.  It is typically manufactured by machining grooves into a mold or directly into the part – or by photolithographic methods in which the wedges are built up in a series of thin layers, each of which is a fraction of the total groove height.

 

There are two ways to simulate a wedge-shaped groove.  In the figure below, curve A is the OPD function to be built into the DOE.  Curve B is the same function, modulus 1 wavelength.  This is the curve that will appear in the GRAY option.  The step function shown in C is an example of one way to approximate the desired wedge shape with four layers applied in turn to the substrate.

 

 

To make the part as shown in C, one would make four separate DMASK plots, with LEVEL equal to 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, and 0.8, and then transfer the picture to a transparency for the photolithographer.

 

A more sophisticated way to prepare the masks is to consider the binary representation of the height at each point with four bits.  The figure below shows how one can superimpose four masks, each representing the area of the fringe with a given bit equal to one, and model 16 discrete steps in the wedge.  The MODE options B0 through B3 select the mask of bits 0 through 3, respectively.  Elements described in this manner are termed binary optics.

 

 

The ZONES option draws only the location of the boundary of each zone.  It is intended as a reference for verifying that the vendor understands the OPD function.  A table is produced showing the location of each zone boundary.

The physical height of the individual grooves must be calculated so that the top level gives rise to exactly one wavelength of OPD shift at the construction wavelength of the DOE.  For transmission DOEs immersed in AIR, this is just the wavelength/(index-1), where index is that of the emulsion of the substrate, while for reflective DOEs it is one-half of the wavelength.  This height is given on the DMASK plot, for reference.  To understand the sign of the pattern, observe that a positive OPD contribution of the DOE advances the wavefront.  Thus, material should be removed from a lens surface or added to a mirror surface if the OPD function is positive, and the opposite if negative.  You may enter a value of -1 for SIGN to flip the OPD function before the zones are plotted.

 

In the event that a DOE is immersed in a medium other than AIR, with index Nimmersed, the blaze height H as given on the plot must be modified.  The height is then given by Himmersed = Hair * (Ndoe - 1.0) / (Ndoe - Nimmersed).

 

The PROFILE option shows a (Y-Z) cross section of the OPD function, modulus 1 wave, at a scale where the fringe height is one inch.  (The optional X input produces an X-Z profile.)  This picture may be used to verify that the sign of the DOE is understood: it shows the zones as either bowl-shaped or hill-shaped, as seen below.

 

 

Since the substrate is on the right in this example, it is clear that the central zone forms a hill, not a bowl.

 

If the aspheric terms of the DOE are all circularly symmetric and the substrate of the DOE has only a circular (or no) clear aperture assigned, then the DMASK zones are themselves perfect circles.  In this common case, the program samples the OPD function at 10,000 points on a single radius and generates the complete circles from these data.  In this case, with the exception of the GRAY option, the GRID entry is ignored, and the calculation is very quick.  The output resolution is then limited primarily by the granularity of the printout device.  If these conditions are not met, the program will sample the entire area of the DOE at a mesh given by GRID.  This feature is more general but takes far longer, and the resolution is just that of the grid.

 

The faster method also prints the number of discrete rings that have been plotted, to help you ascertain whether your technology is capable of producing the part.

 

The OPD mode plots the OPD function, from the axis to the CAO.  It can be useful if you want to check for inflection points.

 

If the surface has a base curve in addition to the DOE, the output from DMASK is a projection of the zone pattern onto the X-Y plane, not looking normal to the surface.

 

If the surface has been declared a multilayer DOE with USS 20, the part is actually made in two sections, with different materials, and with the same zone locations but different blaze heights.  These heights are typically much higher than is the case with a DOE with AIR on one side, and the sections are mounted very close to each other.  The DMASK plot shows the heights before and after the DOE surface.
