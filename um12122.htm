12.1.2.2 Finding the Best Adjustments                                        Next Previous TOC

 

The previous sections have described how to declare a parameter as an adjustment for BTOL – but before you can do that, you have to identify which variable(s) will do the most good.  This section describes a procedure that can help.

 

When a lens is manufactured and the image is not as good as in the nominal design, it is usually the low-order image errors that predominate.  Thus a well-corrected lens will exhibit third-order spherical aberration and axial coma as a result of manufacturing errors, even though both of those errors would be small or zero if the tolerances were small enough.  The most useful adjustments therefore are often those that most strongly affect those aberrations.  A very simple mode of BTOL will examine all reasonable groups that could be used for airspace and decenter adjustments and print a table showing the effectiveness of each.  The effect is given in OPD units per unit of perturbation.

 

To use this feature, enter a file of the form

 

BTOL

ATABLE

GO

 

The program will search for groups that do not violate the many rules that can prevent use of certain variables, such as a global specification within the group.  Only those thicknesses and decenters that are otherwise available to BTOL are considered, although the group size may be any number that does not violate another rule.   If the lens contains any surfaces affected by tilts or decenters, or declared GLOBAL, the groups are not allowed to span those surfaces.

From the first listing we see that the most sensitive group for compensating spherical aberration consists of the elements enclosed by airspaces 4 and 9.  This has the effect of moving surfaces 6 through 9 along the Z axis.  (The thickness of surface 5, which is also an airspace, was not selected by the program because BTOL will not automatically tolerance it – since it follows another airspace that is already toleranced.)

 

From the second group we see that the most sensitive group to control axial coma is again surfaces 6 through 9.  It would be safe to start with these two adjustments for the actual BTOL analysis.  Keep in mind that here we only examine two aberrations, while a realistic adjustment has to compensate for everything else that can go wrong with the image as well.  For that reason, you may have to experiment with other candidates from the above listing, before you settle on a particular adjustment to use.
