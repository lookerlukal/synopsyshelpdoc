Chapter 1

 

1.0 INTRODUCTION

 

This is the SYNOPSYS User’s Manual, the primary reference to SYNOPSYS (SYNthesis of Optical SYStems).  A companion volume, the Tutorial Manual is the place to start if you are just learning to use the program.  The Tutorial is supposed to be printed out and read from start to finish, while this volume is intended as a reference, to be read as needed.

 

This User’s Manual explains all of the command-mode input to the program.  Although SYNOPSYS is intrinsically a command-language program, users will not necessarily be aware of this because it also contains a host of interactive features that let you do much of your work with just a few mouse clicks.  Thus, where this volume explains the syntax of a command, what you see here is exactly what SYNOPSYS sees as a result of your clicking on a toolbar button or a dialog, even though you may not know that syntax yourself.  The great advantage of your learning the command syntax is that it lets you build your own MACro to do anything you want just by running that file.  MACros can be saved to disk, edited, and can even call other MACros in a subroutine-like fashion or in a chain.  See section 13.2 for more information about this important feature.  To help make the power of the command syntax available, most of the interactive dialogs prepare the commands for a MACro, and after running the dialog you just have to load those data (with LMM) into an editor to see what is there, to run it again, or to edit it.

 

As you glance through the pages of this manual, you will see an enormous collection of commands, features, graphics options, and so on, and it may seem like too much to master at first.  But even if you have no previous exposure to SYNOPSYS, or have learned the commands of another optics package, there will be many features of SYNOPSYS that you can operate with little or no instruction.  For example, here is a sample of what you might type in to do a certain task:

 

INCREASE THE THICKNESS OF ELEMENT 2 BY .01

 

Without reading any further you can tell what this instruction means.  If this kind of computer input seems exceptional, you are right; SYNOPSYS is an exceptional program.

 

This and other tasks can be done in more than one way, and you may select the most convenient.  The natural-language sentence given above is one example of input to the AI, or artificial-intelligence portion of SYNOPSYS, a feature that is described in Chapter 15.  Although for many tasks this feature is simple and convenient, the above sentence is by no means the only, or even the best, way to change a thickness.  Just to give you an idea of the flexibility of SYNOPSYS, let me show you some of the many other ways you could do it.  Using AI, you could also enter sentences like

 

CHANGE THICKNESS 6 TO .12345

or        6 TH = 12.6

or        SET THICKNESS NUMBER 6 EQUAL TO -12.4356

or        DECREASE THE TH OF SURFACE NUMBER 6 TO 5.345

 

or any of a great many equivalent sentences which say what you want to do.  You could also use the command-language form

 

CHG

6 TH 1.234

END

 

to change your lens thickness.  Once learned, these commands are fast and powerful.

 

There are still other ways: using the on-screen lens editing feature WorkSheet, described in Chapter 13, you can see the input data for a selected surface in a separate window while viewing the lens drawing in PAD. You will see something like

 

6 RAD 123.456000 TH 12.900000 GTB S

BK7.

 

Then you simply edit any number that you want to change.  Click on the “Update” button, and the lens picture is redrawn showing the new thickness.  Or you can click on the thumb of one of a set of slider bars to the right of the text window and drag it to the left or right.  The parameter changes smoothly, and the lens picture (and image display) changes in step so you can monitor the effects.

 

We are not done yet.  In the PAD program, click on the Lens Edit toolbar button .  Instantly the entire input file for your lens is reformatted and placed into a MACro editor.  Change any number you want in the editor, and then click the “Run” button.  Again, the PAD picture automatically updates showing the new lens.

 

Suppose you want to know how the spherical aberration is affected by that thickness change. Just type

 

 PLOT SPHERICAL ABER AS TH 6 VARIES FROM 3.5 TO 6.0

 

and you have the answer.

 

As you work with SYNOPSYS you will find that it is as powerful as it is flexible.  Take the merit function, for example.  You could, if you wanted to, correct the third-order aberrations of each element individually, in a zoom lens at 10 positions at the same time (or in 6 completely different lenses at the same time, each with 10 zooms).  While controlling the image centroid location, wavefront variance, and the (X,Y,Z) coordinates of the beams forming a holographic optical element.  At grazing incidence.  With a toroidal focal plane.  And so on.  The flexible merit function is one of the most powerful features of SYNOPSYS, since you can control nearly every aspect of the lens or its image, simultaneously.

 

Suppose you want (for some reason!) the back focal distance to equal the index of refraction of surface 16.  Just type:

 

M 0 1 A BACK

S INDEX 16

 

These commands go into the AANT file, described in section 10.3.  You can define your own symbols:

 

@: the thickness of surface 6

RR: RAY 2 0 0 1 SURF

X1: 23.4

 

Then type

 

@ = X1

 

and that thickness changes again, to a value of 23.4.  Type RR and the program traces the axial marginal real ray and prints the path.

 

By now you should see what we mean when we call SYNOPSYS an exceptional program.  Its power, flexibility, and friendliness are unequaled in the industry.  Rather than list all of the state-of-the-art features in this introduction, we suggest that you examine the Table of Contents to get a glimpse of the scope and power of the code.  We recommend that new users also read the companion volume, the Tutorial Manual, to get basic orientation and a sample of the kinds of features you will find in SYNOPSYS.

 

Learning to use SYNOPSYS is like learning to speak a new language: once you have a modest vocabulary and some knowledge of grammar, you can write volumes.  This User's Manual is intended to give you the rules and vocabulary – much of which is remarkably like the English you already know.
