3.3.2.3 Fresnel Surfaces                                Previous Next TOC

 

(These options go into an RLE or CHG file, and may be entered interactively with the dialog SPS.)

 

A Fresnel surface is one whose slope is not given by the derivative of its sagitta.  A common example is the familiar flat plastic lens with molded concentric grooves bearing a local slope angle.  Such lenses give an image of fair quality and are widely used as solar energy collectors.

 

In the general case, one may consider a Fresnel surface to be a collection of infinitely small zones of arbitrary slope impressed on a surface which itself may have arbitrary shape.  SYNOPSYS allows specification of such surfaces, as follows:

 

To describe the Fresnel surface, two lens surfaces are required.  The first of these should be a dummy surface (with no index difference on either side) with a thickness of zero, whose curvature definition applies to the substrate shape of the Fresnel surface.  The immediately following surface is given whatever shape defines the local slope of the Fresnel surface.  The index difference for the lens is entered on this surface, and it is then declared a Fresnel surface with the input

 

 SN FRESNEL.

 

Any curvature option may be utilized for either of these surfaces, including torics and power-series aspherics.  A flat substrate (the first of the two surfaces) with a conic slope would give the usual flat Fresnel lens.  A slope specified by a second surface with CV = 0 and a non-zero G2 term (using the DC2 input) could be used to describe a Fresnel prism, and so on. The Z-intercept of each ray is given by the first surface while the slope is obtained from the second, making this feature completely general.  The two curvatures may be varied in the normal fashion with the optimization program.

 

If a Fresnel surface is reflective, the SN REFLECTOR designation goes on the second of the two surfaces.

 

A Fresnel surface may be redefined as a normal surface merely by subsequently entering any valid curvature specification for the second of the two descriptors.  If a new slope is to be entered on a Fresnel, the new curvature option should be followed by a new SN FRESNEL declaration.

 

Normally, a Fresnel lens actually has three surfaces, when you include the other side of the lens (which is frequently just a flat surface) -- two for the Fresnel side and one for the other.

 

Only the substrate shape will appear on DWG and other plots.

 

One should be aware that when Fresnel lenses are manufactured, the precision of molding is not adequate to maintain coherence of the light from adjacent zones.  The result is that over the aperture of the beam the phase is rather chaotic, and one cannot reliably compute the OPD of any given ray.  The program will generate a value if requested, but this is probably of little meaning in practical cases.

 

A second type of Fresnel surface is also available, using the USS type 3 shape.  This kind of surface does not assume that the zones are of negligible size, but is restricted to a flat substrate.
