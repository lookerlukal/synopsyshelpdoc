5.16 Closest-fitting sphere (ADEF, ADSTAT)                Next Previous TOC

 

The command

 

 ADEF SN { PRINT / PLOT / FRINGES } [ YINCR  [ REF ]] (Analyze DEFormed surface)

 

will calculate the spherical surface that most closely matches a given aspheric defined as a sphere or conic, plus either a power-series surface with the mnemonics DC1, etc. (see 3.3.2.2.2), a Zernike polynomial (see 3.2.2.2.6), or with the surface mnemonics NCZONE or USS (see 3.3.2.6 and 3.3.2.7).  The program samples a grid of 21x21 points over the surface and calculates the volume difference between the surface and a candidate sphere.  The closest sphere is defined as that with the minimum difference, and where all sag differences are positive (material must be removed to generate the aspheric surface).  The program also prints the sag difference between the asphere and the base curve, computed over the the current CAO of the surface, for reference.

 

You may override the default scaling on the Y-axis of the plot with the optional YINCR input.  If this is entered, there will be exactly 5 tic marks on the Y-axis, separated by YINCR.  This is a useful way to produce curves for two designs that may be directly compared, when the default scales for those designs differ.

 

You can also use ADEF to calculate the fit between an aspheric surface and a given reference sphere.  Enter the radius of the sphere as REF in word 5 of the command.  The program will then not search for the best-fitting sphere, but will just analyze the data you have entered.  For a flat reference, enter a radius of zero.

 

To simply show the shape of the current aspheric surface, the SPROFILE is recommended.  To examine the sag contribution of a single G-term, use the GSAG command.

 

The program also calculates and plots the local curvature of the surface in X and Y and finds the peak and RMS departure of the surface from the best-fit sphere.  The latter quantities can be controlled in optimization via the construction parameter aberrations ADIFF and AVOL, and also via CLINK; their values in that case are found in the AI BUFFER.

Notice that the vertex of the spherical match is shifted to the right of the aspheric surface by 0.00045 mm.  This aspheric is on the right side of an element, and the real surface must be to the left of the spherical match so that the curve can first be generated spherical and then material removed to create the desired curve.  The last line tells you how much glass must be removed.  In this case, the closest-fitting sphere has a radius of -28213,2 mm.

 

To analyze the fit to a reference sphere of radius 20.0, one would enter

 

 ADEF 2 PLOT 0 20.0.

 

The optional [ PRINT / PLOT / FRINGES ] choice on the ADEF command produces a listing, a plot plus listing, or the fringes seen in double pass of the sag difference between the real surface and the closest sphere as found by ADEF.  A sample is shown below, followed by the FRINGES version.

A related feature, ADSTAT, will print some statistics related to an aspheric surface.  Input is

 

 ADSTAT SN
