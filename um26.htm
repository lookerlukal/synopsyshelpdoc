2.6 Stops – Pupils – Vignetting                                        Previous Next TOC

 

Rays to be traced by SYNOPSYS are usually specified by fractional object and entrance pupil coordinates.  Because the definition of the entrance pupil is central to most of the features of SYNOPSYS, there are a number of options that can be selected to define its position, size, and shape.  For an introduction to this complex topic, see the Tutorial Manual.  The options are summarized in section 2.6.6.  Once your lens is set up with any kind of pupil definition, you will find that the Pupil Wizard will greatly assist you in finding the best pupil for your needs.  Just type the command MPW to bring up that feature.

 

The default pupil is described by the paraxial quantities YMP1 and YP1, which are shown below.  This pupil is a circle at right angles to the optical axis.  In cases where the input beam is not circular, does not have the same size off axis as on axis, or when the path of the real chief ray does not follow the paraxial chief ray closely, other options are required.  The available options, which are described in detail in the following sections, are listed below.  Most of them may be entered interactively with the SYS dialog or the Pupil Wizard.

 

 

Summary of Pupil Options

 

1. Pupil position options

 

 a. Paraxial pupil, YP1 entered explicitly (this implies a stop surface)

 b. Paraxial pupil, YP1 found by tracing paraxial ray to stop

 c. Real pupil, real YP1 found by tracing real ray to stop

 d. Real pupil + WAP 2 or WAP 3

 

2. Pupil size and shape options

 

 a. Paraxial pupil, YMP1 entered explicitly

 b. Paraxial pupil, YMP1 found by FILLSTOP at stop

 c. WAP 1 option: tilts circular pupil

 d. WAP 2 option: finds real YMP1, XMP1 by real-ray search at stop CAO

 e. WAP 3 option: finds real YMP1, XMP1; and chief ray, not necessarily at stop surface

 f. VSET parameter to shrink, offset pupil for optimization

 g. VFIELD table to scale, offset pupil for analysis or optimization.

 

The simplest pupil is the paraxial pupil.  The geometry describing the parameters YMP1 and YP1 is determined during a paraxial raytrace of the system, which is automatically performed whenever the lens is input or changed.

 

The program determines at that time the paraxial intercept of the full-field chief ray at surface 1; this is the ray that passes through the center of the surface that has been designated the aperture stop.  The intercept height is called YP1, and its value, along with the paraxial marginal ray height, or semi-aperture YMP1, is used to determine where all other rays hit surface 1 as they encounter the lens system.  For an arbitrary ray, the Y-coordinate on the vertex plane of surface 1 is given by

 

 Y = YP1*HBAR + YMP1*YEN.

 

where the notation "HBAR" refers to the fractional field in the meridional (Y-Z) plane, and "YEN" to the fractional aperture of the ray in the Y-direction, a convention followed throughout this manual.  A similar calculation holds in the X-direction, where the quantities involved are GBAR, XP1, XMP1, and XEN, if those quantities are present in the lens.

 

The value of YP1 may be specified in four ways: if a surface is declared a stop surface in the lens file with the mnemonic APS, the value of YP1 is found by the paraxial program.  If no stop has been declared, an explicit value for YP1 may be entered with the object specification, as described in section 3.1.1.  If no value is entered (and no APS declared), then YP1 is equal to zero, and surface number one in effect becomes the stop.  And if a negative surface number is declared with the APS entry, a REAL PUPIL is in effect., and YP1 will be corrected to the real-ray value any time a real ray is traced.

 

If the object specification gives a skew field dimension, the paraxial chief ray coordinate is computed in both X and Y.
