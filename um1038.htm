10.3.8 Center thickness control (ACC, ACM)                                Next Previous TOC

 

Center thicknesses may be controlled automatically with the input

 

 ACC [ TAR WT  [ WINDOW ] ]

 ACM [ TAR WT  [ WINDOW ] ]

 

in the AANT file. ACC activates a control to prevent thicknesses becoming larger than TAR, while ACM prevents them from becoming smaller.  TAR is always a positive number, even if the TH is negative. Only a single target may be specified for each of these monitors, which therefore apply those targets to all varying thicknesses.

 

This is one of several kinds of monitors that operate continuously during optimization.

 

In the event that you wish your thickness variables to stay within a given range – which is usually the case – these controls give you a convenient way to gain that control.  You can also give limits to the variables themselves, but the automatic method has advantages and is recommended where practical.  Thus, if your range is to be no more than 25.4 mm and no less than 2 mm, you could enter in the AANT file

 

ACC 25.4 1 1

ACM 2 1 1.

 

All glass thicknesses declared as variables will be monitored, and the merit function will be penalized if any of them violate the target, with a weight of WT.  The default ACC target is 1 inch, and the default ACM target is 1 lens unit, both with a weight of 1.0.  This is a useful tool to prevent lenses from becoming too thick or thin, especially in the initial stages of a design.  It applies the same limit to all varying thicknesses.  The penalty is applied quadratically up to an error that is equal to WINDOW, and linearly thereafter.

 

Be aware that there are two methods of constraining lens thicknesses.  Hard limits are always assigned to TH variables, and you may change those limits via the TLIMIT directive.  If the limits are violated at any iteration during optimization, the program scales the solution to put the variable exactly at the limit.  Then it can either drop that thickness variable or allow it to keep varying, depending on the status of mode switch 1.  This method works but is sometimes slow since some iterations accomplish very little.  The other method is to assign very wide limits, so they are not often violated, and use the monitors described above to penalize the MF if the thicknesses stray out of the desired range.  This method is activated automatically if switch 55 is turned on when the PANT file is read.  The two methods often return very different results, owing to the chaotic nature of the lens design landscape, and both are worth trying.

 

Individual center thicknesses may also be controlled, either with boundary limits on the variables or by explicit targets in the merit function.  Another option uses the mnemonics

 

 CTC TAR WT SN SN SN …                (Center Thickness Control)

 CTT TAR WT SN SN SN...                (Center Thickness Target)

 

CTC provides a one-sided control similar to that available for edge thickness (see 10.3.7), while CTT gives an explicit target.  Both of these will override any ACC or ACM control that may be in effect.  They may be assigned to air spaces as well as lens elements (unlike ACC and ACM, which only apply to elements).  You may enter more than a single set of surfaces numbers and targets.  Thus,

 

CTC 9 100 2

CTC 6.6 100 3

 

will control airspace 2 and thickness 3, keeping the former no more than 9 and the latter no more than 6.6 lens units.

 

As with edge-thickness aberrations, the values of TAR and WT are always entered positive. TAR is the desired limit (or value of) the center thickness, and WT is the penalty when the value is one unit too large.
