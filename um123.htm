12.3 Statistical Considerations                                        Next Previous TOC

 

In recent years there has been considerable interest in programs that will provide more than merely a table of sensitivities or their inverse as an aid to computing tolerances for a lens (1,2).

 

Central to the success of these programs are two assumptions which must be reasonably valid: the first is that the change of quality of a lens may be described over a useful range by the first and second derivatives of the quality with respect to each variable, and the second is that certain statistical approximations are valid for the lens.

 

The first has been discussed in section 12.2; for most lenses the two derivatives are reasonably adequate provided that they are calculated exactly, at a typical tolerance level, rather than algebraically with the nominal design.  This is the case with BTOL.

 

The second assumption is a consequence of the law of large numbers, which states that the sum of a large number of independent, randomly distributed variables (or functions of them) is given by a bell-shaped probability curve, called the Gaussian, or normal probability density function.  This curve may be described completely by its mean value, μ and its variance, σ.  Under the proper conditions these quantities may be calculated from the corresponding mean and variance of each of the independent functions and are independent of the particular probability distribution of the latter.

 

This says that if we know the mean value, μ, and the standard deviation, σ, of each of the random variables (lens parameters, in our case), we can predict the probability of the quality descriptor exceeding a threshold value.

 

In order for the law of large numbers to be valid, the number need not be particularly large: numerical evaluation indicates that with as few as six or eight variables the departure of the distribution from Gaussian is negligible.  Three other conditions that must be satisfied are called form invariance, independence, and lack of dominance. These require that the shape of the probability distribution of each variable not be a function of the magnitude of the tolerance, that an error in one variable not affect the errors in others, and that many variables contribute more or less equally to the performance degradation.

 

In most lenses these conditions are adequately satisfied, particularly if the variables are properly defined.  For example, if a thickness error causes an airspace error of the opposite sign somewhere else, they are not independent – but the variable may be redefined as the shift of a surface rather than a simple thickness variation, and in this case the requirement of independence is again satisfied. BTOL accommodates this requirement with the GROUP and THC options.

These equations become somewhat simplified if we make the assumption that the mean value of each variable change is zero.  This is no great loss of generality since by properly defining what we mean by the probability distribution of each variable this can always be made true.  This subject is discussed more fully below.

 

In the linear case, such as when tolerancing the boundary conditions BORE, MAG, and DIS, the result is that the mean value of the change in these quanfities is always zero, and the standard deviation, which is just the root of the variance, is the amount of degradation that will be exceeded by 15.9 percent of the cases.  The two-sigma error will be exceeded by only 2.3 percent of all cases.

 

For the image quality descriptors, which are nonlinear with respect to the variables, the result is that the mean value of the change of Q is not necessarily zero. BTOL sums the expected mean and the multiple deviation of ΔQ, and uses this value as the quantity whose magnitude is not to exceed the desired tolerance.  The “multiple deviation” here is the second word on the BTOL line, giving the confidence level as one sigma, two sigma, etc., or the equivalent quantity derived from the percentage criterion, if that is input.

 

From these results we see that the only parameter of interest to BTOL, for preparing the budget, is the standard deviation σ of each of the variables.  The budget is prepared in such a way that a required value of σ is obtained for all quantities to be toleranced.  In order to relate this to an actual shop tolerance, however, it is necessary to know something about the statistics that apply to the particular variable.  In other words, we must know what tolerance to specify in order to guarantee that the variable will have the required variance.

 

There are three kinds of statistics that are of interest in this regard.  The first is the case when the variable may be described by a single number, such as radii, thicknesses, indices, etc.  This is a one-dimensional problem and the only issue is the relative probability of the variable having a particular value within its tolerance range.  In some cases we would expect that the variable would be equally likely to appear anywhere in the range, and in others it may be more often near the center or the ends.  To resolve such differences requires detailed records of how well a particular optical shop meets the tolerance requirements on the lenses that it has processed.  Fortunately, for our purposes this is not very important.

 

It is easily shown that the standard deviation of a variable with a uniform probability distribution within a range of plus or minus E is equal to E / (square root of 3).  For such a variable the proper tolerance is therefore σ * 1.732 where σ is the desired multiple deviation value derived from the budget calculation.  If a variable is always found at one end of its range or the other, the proper value of E would be equal to σ.  The difference between these two cases is less than a factor of two and you can easily convert from one to the other if you know the details of the distribution of a particular variable.  For example, BTOL assumes a uniform distribution for the one-dimensional variables and the printed tolerance is therefore σ * 1.732 To convert this to the other distribution merely divide by 1.732.

 

A second kind of distribution is two-dimensional, as in the case of element decenters; here the center of the lens may be displaced in both X and Y

But this considers only decenters near the Y axis.  Ideally, the effects of X decenters should be evaluated and subject to the same statistics as the Y decenters, but to do so would increase the computation costs by nearly 50% (since tilts should be treated as well).  Instead, let us consider two possibilities: the first is that X decenters are equally serious sources of degradation of images in the meridional plane, and the second is that they are not at all serious, and only affect images in the X-Z plane.  In actual practice the effects are usually in between these two extreme cases, so this analysis will bound the problem.

 

In the first case, where decenters in any direction are equally serious sources of quality degradation, the problem essentially has become one dimensional, as in the uniform case, except that the statistics involve the root of two instead of three. In the second case, if we integrate over all possible angular orientations of the distribution pictured above, the standard deviation of the decenter turns out to be σ = E / 2.0.

 

The point of all of this is to show that the tolerances are not particularly sensitive to the details of the probability distributions for each of the lens variables.  In order to be conservative, BTOL multiplies the sigma value by the root of three for one-dimensional variables and by the root of two for decenters.

 

The cases of wedge, element tilt, and irregularity are similar to that of decenter, except that the one-sigma probability of the error is not increased over the linear case by virtue of a circular distribution.  That is, the error itself has a uniform distribution and only its orientation is circular.  Again, to minimize computation costs, BTOL assumes that the error is equally serious in all orientations, and utilizes a scaling factor equal to the root of three.
