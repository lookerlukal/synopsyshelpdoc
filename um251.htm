2.5.1 Reflections                                                                Previous Next TOC

 

The paths of rays are normally computed through a lens system from left to right.  Two kinds of rays are considered: NORMAL, and STRANGE.  Normal rays proceed to the right through a system with no reflections, or an even number of reflections.  When a reflecting surface is encountered, the ray then proceeds from right to left.  That surface must be declared REFLECTIVE, and thicknesses usually become negative thereafter, following the path of the light.  The light is then said to be in negative space.  If another reflection is encountered, the direction again reverses.  See 6.3 regarding the signs of ray angles.

 

STRANGE rays proceed to the right through a negative space, and to the left through a positive space. This kind of ray is usually produced by a grazing-incidence reflection.  With either kind of ray, a reflection is designated by declaring the surface a reflector.  The rule regarding intersections, however, is different for the two.  See the next section.

 

Users of previous versions of SYNOPSYS (before version 12) should note this change: Indices of refraction are always positive starting with version 12, regardless of ray direction.  Reflectors are declared explicitly, as mentioned above, and are no longer implied by the sign of the index as in earlier releases.  SYNOPSYS can read RLE files prepared in earlier versions and will automatically convert the signs where needed so as to conform to the new rules.

 

In the event that rays must enter the system from the right instead of the left, the system parameter LRAYS must be included in the RLE file.  To restore the default, use RRAYS.

 

It is also possible to declare a surface in negative space a PSPACE surface.  The program will then revert to the usual rules for intersections and rendering.  See that link for details.
