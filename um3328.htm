3.3.2.8 Diffractive optical elements (DOEs)       Previous Next TOC

 

(These options go into an RLE or CHG file, and may be entered interactively with the dialog SPS.)

 

SYNOPSYS has two forms of diffractive optical elements (DOEs).  A simple form with only rotationally-symmetric shape and OPD coefficients is given by surface types USS 16, 20, 25, 26, and 27, described in Section  3.3.2.7.  A more general form, with a variety of expansion polynomials, is described below.

 

This kind of diffractive optical element may be defined with the input

 

SN DOE CWAV HIN VNUM

[ RNORM RNORM ]

 

Where SN is the surface to be declared a DOE, CWAV is the wavelength at which the OPD function is to be defined, and HIN is the index of the emulsion.  The latter is used for preparing the fringe height multiplier to be displayed on the DMASK plot.  The VNUM gives the Abbe number of the emulsion.  The blaze angle is calculated from the local fringe spacing and the value of HIN, and the diffraction efficiency is given by the difference between the construction wavelength and index, and the same during playback.

 

The value of VNUM is given by

 

VNUM = (N(primary) - 1) / (N(short) - N(long))

 

where the three index values are taken from the wavelengths assigned to the lens as given by the color order.  It will equal the Abbe number of the material only if those wavelengths are the C, d, and F lines.

 

This type of DOE is modeled with two surfaces, as are HOEs; see the discussion at that link for details.  An AR coating may be assigned to the second of the two.

 

The DOE is modeled by SYNOPSYS as a special case of a HOE; the program puts points P1 and P2 at the same location so the DOE itself has no optical power, and then accepts OPD coefficient terms in the form A11 … .  For DOEs, these terms are interpreted as follows:

 

[ A11 G16 G18 G21 G25 G31 ]

[ A12 G17 G19 G20 G2 G23 G24 ]

[ A13 G26 G27 G28 G29 G30 G32]

+ G(20)Y**2

where R is the radial distance from the axis.

 

Unlike the HOE and USS features, this legacy DOE form does not accept an ORDER parameter.

 

This polynomial is of the same form as the power-series aspheric equation used for lens surfaces, described in Section 3.3.2.2.2, except they start at G16 instead of G1 to allow storage for the other HOE parameters.

 

The Zernike terms are defined in Section 3.3.2.2.6.

To zero out all of these terms, enter XDD 0 with no other arguments.  Note that this form is different from that used for the USS type 8 surface: the latter requires a surface number, while the data for a DOE do not.

 

With this form, rays hitting at or inside the zone of radius given by XD 18 are diffracted by the function in terms XD1 through XD15, while those hitting outside of this radius are diffracted by terms XD19 through XD33.  The DOE therefore can have two different powers, one in the center zone and the other in the outside zone.  (It is also possible for the substrate to have two different powers in two zones, if that shape is declared to be a USS type 8.  In this case one would probably want the zone radius to be the same for the DOE as for the USS.  But any valid surface shape can be assigned, and it is not necessary to utilize the USS substrate shape.)

 

Term XD16 gives a normalizing radius for the expansion.  If it is nonzero, the radial distance from the vertex is divided by this number before the OPD is calculated.

 

Term XD32 is a phase offset, calculated by the program, that adjusts the phase of the outer zone to match that of the inner at the zone boundary.  This avoids a sharp break in the OPD at that location.  You should not edit this value, since the program recalculates it.

 

You may enter an optional normalizing radius in RNORM.  If this is nonzero, the program will divide the aperture location of a given ray by this number before calculating the phase shift that is due to G or S-terms.   (The XDD form places this factor in data location XD16, and ignores the RNORM input.)

 

Input is normally in units of OPD, or cycles of phase shift, but you can enter the data in lens units if you precede all S-term entries with the input

 

WLENS

 

 

 WOPD (the default) specifies entry in OPD units.

 

The Surface Shape dialog (reached from SPS or from the WorkSheet via the "Surface Data" toolbar button) will lead to a subdialog where you can convert from lens units to OPD units and vice-versa.

 

You can see the shape of the OPD function with the DSAG feature.

 

A DOE may be thought of as a circular grating applied to a lens surface.  The grating is usually very coarse by grating standards, and is blazed at the construction wavelength so as to give nearly 100% efficiency.  At other wavelengths, the blazing is not perfect, but the grating equation determines the diffraction angle, rather than the blaze angle, and for that reason long wavelengths are bent more than short – in contrast to the case with refractive optics where short wavelengths invariably encounter a larger index and are bent more.  Thus, with diffractive optics it is possible to balance the two and to correct color with only a singlet.

 

For a discussion of the manufacture of DOEs, see the section on the DMASK command.

 

Note that reflective DOEs are quite practical, and are treated as coated reflective HOEs.  This means that the DOE itself is a transmissive element, rather than reflective, and the reflection occurs on the substrate, which must be declared a REFLECTOR.  For a reflective DOE, the emulsion index should be 1.0, since the DOE itself will be aluminized and the index is not material.  (The "substrate" is always the second of the two DOE surfaces in this discussion, even if the DOE is on the second side of an element and the physical substrate is actually on a lower-numbered surface.)

 

All DOEs (except the USS 16 type mentioned above) require two surfaces, as do HOEs.  The first is declared the DOE, with no index change, and the second is the substrate.  The latter may be assigned any shape option except Fresnel, and is given the index of the substrate as well as any clear aperture size that may be needed.  Tilts and decenters, on the other hand, are assigned to the DOE itself, not to the substrate.

 

 

DOE OPD parameters may be varied in the same way as HOE parameters (although you are cautioned not to alter any of the construction parameters other than the coefficients.)  These are always assigned to point P1, and no coefficients should be declared on point P2.  The S-term coefficients may be varied as follows:

 

 G16 through G36 will vary terms S1 through S21.

 

G-terms are varied in a similar manner:

 

 Variables G16 through G31 will vary DOE terms G16 through G31.

 

And the XDD coefficients follow the same rule:

 

 G16 through G48 will vary terms XD1 through XD33.

 

If your DOE uses Zernike coefficients, the variables are a little more complicated.  Coefficients Z1 through Z17 are varied by input of the form

 

 VY SN LHG 16

to

 VY SN LHG 32.

 

Coefficients Z18 through Z34 use variables RHG 16 through 32.  Terms Z35 is varied with LGH 33, and term Z36 with RHG 33.

 

Example DOE:

 

Here is the input for a MACro that will design a color-corrected singlet to be molded in acrylic plastic.

 

RLE

ID SINGLET DOE

WAVL CDF

OBB 0 1 1

1 RAD 10 TH .3 GLM 1.491 61.4

2 DOE .6328 1.491 65.2

A11

3 YMC 0 TH 15

4

END

PANT

VY 1 ASPH

VY 2 G 16

VY 2 G 18

VY 2 G 21

END

AANT

GSR 1 10 3 2

GSR 1 5 3 1

GSR 1 5 3 3

GNR 1 1 3 2 1

END

SNAP

SYNO 10

 

 

In this input we declared the DOE on surface 2, and put a YMC solve on surface 3, which will be the substrate of the DOE.  This is one way to control the first-order properties of the lens; in this case the image will be in focus on surface 4 at a distance of 15 inches from the DOE.  The radius could also be input explicitly and varied as usual.

 

The DOE was assigned power-series OPD terms (which are all zero to start with) with the A11 input.  The format of this input is the same as for HOEs.  Now it is possible to vary these terms in optimization in order to correct color along with anything else in the merit function.  If the lens is to be molded in plastic, this is a good place to use aspherics as well as the DOE, since the finished lens does not increase in price with this added complexity (although the mold will be more expensive).

 

The OPD terms of order 2, 4, and 6 in powers of the zone height are varied with terms G 16, 18, and 21.  The polynomial is the same as that described by input of the form DC1, etc., (see section 3.3.2.2.2).  These may be varied, with DOE aspheric terms G1 through G16 varied by changing variables G 16 through G 31 in the DOE.  See section 10.2.

 

It is simple to check the spatial frequency of the DOE:  Here is a MACro that uses the MAP feature to prepare a table showing the frequency in cy/mm at 25 points distributed over surface 2

 

DEFAULT.MAC

--- MAP HSFREQ OVER PUPIL ON SURF 2

--- FGRID POINT 0 0

--- RGRID CREC 5 5

--- ACTUAL

--- DIGITAL

--- PRINT

 

 0.000000E+00  0.000000E+00  0.122436E+02  0.000000E+00  0.000000E+00

 

 0.000000E+00  0.455905E+01  0.163926E+01  0.455905E+01  0.000000E+00

 

 0.122436E+02  0.163926E+01  0.235583E-05  0.163926E+01  0.122436E+02

 0.000000E+00  0.000000E+00  0.122436E+02  0.000000E+00  0.000000E+00

 

Here we see that the DOE requires a maximum of just over 12 cy/mm at the edge of the beam.  To see the locations on the DOE where these data apply, you could use the ”PRINT FULL” option of MAP.
