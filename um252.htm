2.5.2 Intersections                                                                Previous Next TOC

 

In tracing rays through a system containing second-order surfaces (spheres and conic sections) there are in general two intersections of a ray with the surface.  The correct intersection for a given application may be controlled by a user specification that assigns to the surface one of three possibilities: AUTO, NEAR, or FAR.  The default is AUTO, and under this rule the program selects the intersection that is geometrically closest to the vertex of the surface, for normal rays, and the other intersection for strange rays.  This rule has proved useful for system such as the Schwarzschild lens, where a portion of the beam consists of normal rays, and the rest of strange rays, as shown below.  It also works for most grazing-incidence applications.

 

 

For cases where the default intersection is not the one desired, you may select NEAR or FAR.  As the selection implies, NEAR always selects the intersection nearest the vertex, and FAR the other.

 

In the event that the portion of the surface to be used is entirely past the hemisphere point, an additional specification that defines the surface itself as NEAR or FAR should be entered.  The input format for these options is given in section 3.3.1.2, and the default is NEAR.

 

Note that the paraxial programs always consider only the near vertex of a surface.  If an intersection or surface is defined FAR, the paraxial quantities such focal length, etc., might have little meaning if it is the other vertex that is actually used.

 

Some of the curvature options consist of a base curve, which can be a sphere or conic section, with an added deformation, such as a power-series, Zernike, or spline deformation.  The intersection rule only applies to the base curve in these cases.  The program selects the intersection with the deformed surface that is closest to that with the base curve.
