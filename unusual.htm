Unusual materials are listed in two sections: a special broadband list in which the material mnemonic is valid over the entire useful wavelength range of the material, and a second list with more materials in which each mnemonic is valid over but a portion of the range.  If you attempt to use a material from this catalog at a wavelength that is outside of the valid range, a warning message will be displayed.

 

Be aware that we have relied on data from numerous vendors and from published measurements of the refractive index in compiling this glass table.  Unfortunately, many of the sources do not agree with each other, and we have had to select from the differing measurements those that appear to be the most reliable.  We have generally preferred more recent measurements, or those from the most prestigious institutions.  But for critical applications, we recommend that you contact the vendor from whom the material will be purchased and get the most recent information that they can provide.  The index can then be adjusted with the MELT function, if the wavelengths are within the visual range, or by simply entering the updated index values into the lens if they are not.

The index value for germanium is difficult to pin down; different vendors report different values. We have selected what appear to be reliable data, properly measured, as a function of wavelength and temperature.  But a different batch from a different vendor will likely yield different measurements.  You are advised to use the catalog values for roughing out your lens, and then ask for precise measurements from the selected vendor on projects where the exact index is important.

 

Customizing the U catalog

 

New materials are constantly being developed, and it can happen that a material you need is not yet in the catalog.  Read the instructions below to learn how you can edit the catalog and add new materials yourself.  Be warned, however, the formats are strict, and if you make a mistake the catalog may not work anymore.

Line 2 contains the range of wavelengths over which the index interpolation is valid.  In this case from 0.405 um to 1.083 um.

 

Line 3 is the wavelength range over which transmission data are available; in this case 0.38um to 0.8 um, and the thickness at which those data are measured, in this example 3 mm.

 

Line 4 has the six interpolation coefficients (for selections blank and S).  You will likely not use the other formulas. These data are in a strict format: 6E13.7.  (Other options use more than a single line.)

 

Line 5 has the dn/dt and CTE data: for selection B, this consists of three dn/dt values followed by the three wavelengths at which those values apply.  Word 7 is the CTE.  For selection C, this line has only the CTE.  The 6-term form uses a polynomial published by Schott and Ohara; you will likely not have those coefficients for your new material.

 

Line 6 has the number of transmission values to follow.

 

The remaining lines have, for each value, the wavelength and internal transmission value.

 

If you elect to edit the file, be sure to keep a backup copy in case your additions do not work.  Test your new material by looking a the U catalog in the glass map (MGT), and locate the name.  Then add that material to a lens and verify that the index values are correct.  Then use the PGA sn command and check that everything looks proper.  Finally, in the glass map dialog, navigate to your new material and click the Properties button.  If all looks proper, click the List button on the Properties dialog and check the listing in the Command Window.

 

When all these tests look good, your new material is ready to use!

 

We must caution you that every time you install a program update, the U catalog will be replaced and your new material will vanish.  For that reason, we strongly recommend that when your material is properly working, by all means send us a copy of your added data.  We will add it to the base copy of the U catalog and it will always be available thereafter.

 

You may wonder where to find the index interpolation coefficients.  Check the manufacturer's data sheet to see if they are already there.  If not, enter from six to 20 wavelengths and the index value into SYNOPSYS using the GFIT option of the RLE file.  The program will print the six coefficients for the old Schott formula.  Copy those data exactly and add them to your new material description.  Here is an example.  We load the lens 1.RLE and run the MACro
