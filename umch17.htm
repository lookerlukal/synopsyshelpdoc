17.0 POLARIZATION

 

The material presented in the previous sections of this manual embodies what is known as the default assumption of geometrical optics; the wavefront has uniform (or apodized) amplitude and constant polarization state.  For the majority of optics problems these are excellent assumptions.  Diffraction analysis in this case uses simple scalar diffraction theory, which says that if two waves are in phase they add, and if out of phase they cancel, ignoring the angle between them.

 

But there are cases where polarization, and the related problem of vector diffraction, play a role.

 

Examples of such cases include the following:

 

·very fast systems
·roof prisms
·where coatings are to be modeled
·where polarization is important.

 

In very fast systems, the default scalar diffraction theory becomes inaccurate.  Here one must consider that energy from opposite sides of the pupil does not undergo interference in the usual manner; two waves can interfere completely only if their E-vectors are parallel, which is in general not the case.

 

 

In the sketch above, the E -vectors in the plane of the page are not parallel for the two rays shown, while the vectors out of the page are parallel.  The former therefore undergo only partial interference, while for the latter it is complete – and this affects the diffraction pattern.  A more accurate vector-diffraction analysis involves decomposing the E-fields into three mutually orthogonal components and performing the diffraction integral three times, adding the results incoherently.  The image so found is somewhat larger than that found by scalar theory and for this example would not exhibit circular symmetry either.  SYNOPSYS can perform the required vector-diffraction evaluation.

 

In roof prisms, with total internal reflection, there is a pronounced change in the polarization of the light upon reflection.  (A lesser change occurs if the roof surfaces are coated with a reflecting metallic film.)  In the former case the size of the diffraction pattern is approximately doubled in the direction transverse to the roof edge, and the MTF is cut by about half in that direction.  Clearly, this is a serious effect, since it occurs even in systems in which polarization plays no obvious role, where the OPD errors are all zero, and the roof angle is exactly 90 degrees.

 

Most of the coatings commonly applied to lens and mirror surfaces affect the polarization of the light, and the performance of a given coating may be affected by the polarization that occurs at a previous surface.  If the characteristics of a system are to be accurately modeled, it is therefore sometimes necessary to model the coating effects during raytracing.  The efficiency of a coating is a function of obliquity as well as wavelength, and as a result, if the system has steep incident angles, the uniformity of the illumination of the exit pupil may be altered by the coatings – leading to unintended apodization and a change in the diffraction pattern and MTF.

 

Since all of the effects described above influence the polarization of the light to some degree, they are of critical importance in cases where the system is intended to measure polarization.  The unwanted polarization derived from reflections and coating characteristics may collectively be called "instrumental polarization", and it is a first-order error in such systems.

 

A simple way to emulate some of the effects of vector diffraction on the image is with the EVD option in the lens file.  This gives a fair approximation of the loss of resolution associated with a very fast beam, but does not treat the rotation arising from a roof prism, for which a complete polarization  analysis is required.  This is still a scalar calculation, if polarization mode is not turned on, and it models the loss of influence at steep angles via a suitable change in apodization over the pupil.
