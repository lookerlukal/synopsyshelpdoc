12.1.1.1 Cemented Elements and BTOL                                        Next Previous TOC

 

When you have modeled a cement layer as an explicit surface with very small thickness, it must be declared with the CEMENT option in the BTOL input.  When declared in this way, the cement layer is not given a thickness or wedge tolerance (since the cement is presumably well applied and controlled).  The element following the cement layer, the second half of the doublet, is not given a separate tilt or decenter tolerance, but the final surface of that element is given a wedge tolerance.  As a result of these operations, the second element of a doublet is assumed to tilt and decenter in a manner that follows the wedge of the first element, and have a wedge error of its own in addition, while the cement layer itself is considered perfect.

 

If a radius changes in such a way that the very close surfaces become feathered, the program will automatically increase the thickness of the cement layer as necessary to avoid such feathering, both in BTOL and in MC (which would otherwise vignette the ray bundle under these conditions).

 

The CEMENT option is especially important if you turn on switch 14 (to align successive surfaces with their wedge errors in opposite directions when you run MC).  Otherwise the program will treat the cement layer as just another element and will align its wedge opposite to that of the elements on either side, as though it were a triplet.  Then the two outer elements will be aligned so that their wedges add instead of canceling.  Also, without this option, the cement would be given a wedge by BTOL that would likely give severe vignetting when later modeled by MC.

The tolerance budget for this case will include both the wedge and roll tolerances, if you declare the RL1 variables.  They are both part of the budget, even though they describe almost the same thing: the wedge tolerance may be specified to the shop to guide them in centering each element, while the RL1 tolerances are a guide to how accurately they must be aligned for cementing.

 In this case, the triplet was defined with contacted surfaces rather than cemented.  Now, when a wedge is applied to the first element, it follows the default rules for wedges, which do not consider what follows.  This means that the wedge on the first element is described by a tilt of the second side, with a group size of 1.  So the element contacted to its right models the somewhat unrealistic situation where it also has a wedge, of the opposite sign, and the following elements remain where they were.  We do not recommend this approach.

 

If you do not want to create and declare explicit cement layers, you should set the wedges to EXACT, and declare the first side of each element with the RL1 parameter.  This will be a better model for reality, but it is not as good as the cemented case, because you do not get usable wedge tolerances this way.  Remember, changing a contacted pair into a pair with explicit cement can be done with a single mouse click in the WorkSheet.
