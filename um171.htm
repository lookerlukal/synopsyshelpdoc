17.1 Polarization Raytracing                                                Next Previous TOC

 

To deal with the effects described above, it is necessary to consider the polarization of light during raytracing, and to provide a means to model the coatings on lens surfaces.  SYNOPSYS provides options for this purpose.

 

Polarization raytracing requires first a definition of the polarization state of the object.  Although simple for a plane wave, this becomes more complicated for finite objects, since the definition of polarization is then not so obvious.

 

In general, the polarization state of any ray may be completely defined by resolving the current E-vector(s) into two orthogonal components, and assigning the proper amplitude and phase to each.  Since any two orthogonal axes may be taken, there is no preferred, or correct, choice; the vectors chosen are selected by the program at each surface according to what is most convenient at that surface.

 

It is not sufficient, however, to define, say, a "Y-polarization", as consisting solely of E-vectors in the Y-direction.  If a ray propagates at an angle to the Z-axis, its E-vectors will in general have (X,Y,Z) components.  The convention selected for SYNOPSYS treats a polarized object as an array of electric dipoles, of given orientation, and evaluates the field vectors of each ray in accordance with the dipole field in that direction.  Thus, for example, If an object were circularly polarized, one would define the object array as the superposition of two dipole arrays, one oriented in the X-direction, and the other in the Y-direction, with 90 degrees phase difference between the two.  But the two dipoles at a given object point do not give rise to orthogonal E-vectors on a given ray, except for the ray propagating along the Z-axis, so they must be resolved into two orthogonal E-vectors at each surface.
