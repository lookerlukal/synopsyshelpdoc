8.8 Mapping Function                                 Next Previous TOC

 

The MAP feature will evaluate of a variety of properties of the lens system as a function of either field of view or location within the entrance pupil.  An elementary use of this feature might be to show the spot diagram at several field points, but several less obvious uses are of interest: you might map the local HOE grating frequency at many points in the pupil at a given object coordinate.  You could see a picture showing the local angle of incidence of, say, the chief ray at a particular surface over many field points, or map the OPDs of a grid of rays over the pupil.

 

Output may be plotted either in digital or analog fashion.  You can store a set of mapped data, create a second set, and plot the differences between the two sets – thereby mapping the convergence difference in a binocular system, for example.  Mapping positions may correspond to actual coordinates at a surface or the image – or to exploded positions that are a function of initial coordinates rather than final ones.  Up to 20,000 rays may be requested and processed.

 

MAP may be run interactively from the dialog MMA.  There are a total of 19 items that may be mapped.  These can take numerous directives that control what is displayed and in what way.  The table below summarizes the allowed combinations.  (Y means the data are required, “~” means optional, and N means not allowed.  The column P234 refers to ray grid geometry other than square.  These are similar to the PUP 2, 3, and 4 options for footprints.  The SPOT , IPDISTORTION, and VAR plots always utilize a square grid.)

 

Note: we do not require SYNOPSYS users to study this chart.  It is provided only for your reference.  The MMA dialog does the work for you.  As you select items in that dialog, other items are enabled or disabled according to the rules, and you don’t have to worry about them.  After that, the command LMM will load the command sequence into a MACro editor, all properly formatted, and you can save that job as a MACro if you want to.

There are two basic forms of MAP: over the field of view (FOV) and over the pupil (PUPIL).  (An additional form, described below, maps the surface SAG over a surface.)  These two kinds can be requested for those arguments listed above.  For example, to map the footprint on surface 6 as a function of field point, you might enter

 

MAP FOOTPRINT OVER FOV ON SURFACE 6

 

and follow this with the appropriate selection of parameters.

 

Numerous optional data items are listed above, and each has a default that depends on the other options that are selected.

 

Some requests also produce a line of statistics at the top of the plot, giving the maximum, minimum, and average of the data plotted.

 

The mnemonic QMAP runs MAP in quiet mode, where no diagnostic or analysis results are displayed.  If you are not confident that your input is meaningful, it is best to run with MAP instead of QMAP, so you can see error and warning messages on the Command Window.

 

Notice that a particular surface number must be given for some requests, may be given for others, and may not be given for yet others.  Where optional, the default is the image surface.  Where required, any surface number except the image surface is permitted.  The easiest way to sort out the possibilities is with the MMA dialog, where each selection affects which of the others are enabled.  The possibilities are distinguished in the above general format description.  In most cases, if you map over the field, you can specify only a single ray (with RGRID POINT ...)  and if you map over the pupil you can specify only a single field point (with FGRID POINT ...)  Exceptions are the SPOT, FOOTPRINT, TRANS, RMS, IPDIST and VAR plots, which are plotted over the field but where the RGRID data give the number of the ray grid to trace.

 

The mnemonics are:

 

ZZ

is the tangent of the local X-Z angle of a ray after reflection or refraction.

 

HH

is the tangent of the local Y-Z angle of a ray after reflection or refraction.

 

UNI

is the incidence angle, in degrees, of a ray at the requested surface, measured from the surface normal.

 

FOOT

gives a footprint of rays at a surface.  If the ray selection CREC is selected, the rays are traced over a square grid, bounded at the unit circle unless the lens has been declared RPUPIL.  If switch 90 is ON, the ray grid will be hexapolar instead of square.  The other ray patterns are not necessarily square and ignore switch 90.

 

XA, YA, and ZA

 

are the actual (X,Y,Z) local coordinates of a ray intersection at a surface.

 

SAG

is the same as ZA, except that it can be evaluated over the whole surface, at the current CAO, instead of over those locations where a set of rays hits that surface.  To exercise this option, use the form “OVER SURFACE...”.

 

HFREQ, GFREQ, HSFREQ

 

 

give the local HOE and GRATING frequency at the designated ray point.  The frequency is taken at the last HOE or GRATING encountered by the ray, but the plot may be on any surface.  The result is always in cy/mm, regardless of lens units.  The form HSFREQ gives the frequency at the surface; the others give the frequency normal to the fringe plane.

 

SPT

SPOT

These map the spot diagram.  The number of rays traced in each color is adjusted to represent the spectral weighting, if a multicolor analysis is requested.  The program also draws, for reference, the size of the Airy diffraction disk, calculated for the major color and the actual F/number in X and Y at the center image point.  This may be defeated by turning on switch 80.  Color output is produced if switch 35 is on.  Realistic colors may be obtained by turning on switch 85 as well.  This evaluation is always performed at the final image.  Switch 90 will produce a hexapolar ray grid if the ray pattern CREC is selected.

 

To see the spot diagrams in reasonable colors, the display must show a black background.  In that case it can combine rays of differing colors using the logic red + blue + green = white.  If the display has a white background, on the other hand, then a good image would show up as white on white, and would be hard to see.  For this reason, the program does not then combine colors, but instead overlays the colors, making the last-drawn color determine the color of any one pixel.  If switch 85 is on, it will draw a black box behind the fiducials first, and the combined colors are then shown correctly even with a white background.

 

If the display is shown with a black background and you click the Print button, the program by default switches to a white background, to avoid showing a black page with thin colored lines – which are usually hard to see – and to avoid wasting a large amount of expensive toner.

 

The BINOCULAR option has its own rules: there is no black box behind the picture in that case.  But if you want to see your SPT map with combined colors, you can still do it.  Simply turn on switch 92, and ask for a black background, before you click the Print button.  That switch makes the printout honor the black background setting.

 

[R][H]DIST

selects one of four types of distortion analysis.  The basic DIST gives the difference in X and Y between the final chief-ray intercept and the ideal point, based on the magnitude of GIHT, divided by GIHT.  This is a normalized distortion, as is that given by DIS.  Skew object points are scaled by the X-GIHT or by the ratio XP0/YP0.

 

If the RDIST form is used, the output is in lens units, rather than normalized, for FOCAL lenses, and in angular units otherwise.

 

If the HDIST form is used, the analysis ignores the value of GIHT and scales the result to the field point given by the REFHBAR input.  This is appropriate when paraxial mode is turned off, when the axis ray intercept is nonzero, or when you wish to define the image at a certain field point as the “zero distortion” point, and examine the departure of other field points from that measure.  For example, if your lens shows strong 3rd order distortion, you might give a reference HBAR of 0.7, which says, in effect, that the magnification has been altered so that the 0.7 field point goes where it does with no distortion.  Then the MAP analysis will show how the magnification of other points in the field varies from that value – which is just the distortion of those points with respect to that magnification.  Why bother?  Well, the resulting distortion is only a fraction of the paraxial value, and if you have to meet a spec, this is a quite honest way to do so.

 

the RHDIST form is a combination of the above two options.

 

OPD

gives the optical path difference of the requested ray.

 

PUPIL

plots a map of ray coordinates in the entrance pupil over the FOV.  This form is always shown in exploded view.

 

TRANS

There are two forms of transmission evaluation in MAP, depending on whether the analysis is over a grid of field points or a grid of pupil points.

 

If the FGRID specifies a set of field points (OVER FOV), the result gives the transmission at each field point when the requested grid of rays is traced.  In this case, the calculation takes into account reflection losses, if polarization mode is in effect, as well as vignetting losses.  Apodization is always considered, but absorption losses are not considered in this case.

 

If the analysis is for a set of rays over the pupil from a single object point, then the transmission is calculated for each ray individually, and all losses are considered, including absorption, along that ray path.

 

Other features that can evaluate transmission are TN, GTRANS or RTRANS, which are conveniently found in the menu MTR.

 

POLAR

plots the polarization. See chapter 17.  This feature lets you monitor how the polarization changes from surface to surface.  Just include the “ON SURFACE NB” with the input.  It requires that polarization mode be on.

 

Note that the program shows a projection of the polarization state on the local X-Y plane.  It does not show the Z-component, if any.  If the ray arrives at a surface at a steep angle, this causes the X-Y projection to appear foreshortened.

 

RMS

The number of rays traced in this case is taken from the RGRID input with a ray count equal to the product of NX and NY.  If fewer than 100 rays result, the program substitutes a ray number of 2000.  This form is always shown in exploded view.

 

VAR

calculates the wavefront variance over the field.  The ray grid is controlled in the same was as for the RMS analysis.  This form is always shown in exploded view.

 

IPDIST

plots distortion after taking into account the location of the diffraction image point.  This is intended primarily for extremely demanding applications where distortion must be controlled to a fraction of a micron.  The image point is the location of the peak of the diffraction pattern measured from the reference ray, which is either the chief ray in the requested color or in the primary color for multicolor analysis.  Even if the chief rays are mapped without any distortion, if the image point varies over the field, the user will perceive distortion in the image.

 

The program finds the image point for each field angle to be analyzed, subtracts the image point location at the on-axis image, and compares the result to an ideal location on a grid where the distance from center to edge is the difference in the chief-ray intercepts from the center to the top of the field.  The difference between the two is then taken as the distortion for that field point.

 

If the lens shows a constant offset from the chief-ray intercept (because of coma that is constant over the field, for example) this analysis shows that the overall distortion may be very small since all field points are displaced by the same amount.

 

The calculation only makes sense if the image is corrected to within about one or two waves; otherwise the image point may not correspond to a peak of the diffraction pattern.  (It actually refers to the point where the wavefront variance is minimized, which is at the peak for small errors.)
 

For more information about the image point, see the section on the VARIANCE command.

 

GSAG

This calculates the SAG of the surface over a grid of points and returns the locations in global coordinates.  It can be used to create a point cloud that can be input to some CAD programs for modeling the shape.

 

The FGRID entry defines the grid pattern in object space (the field grid) for mapping over the FOV, and may also define the object point for mapping over the pupil.  Input is shown above.

 

RECT

defines a rectangular FOV with NHBAR points in the vertical (Y) field and NGBAR in the skew (X) field.  The default takes 5 points in the meridional plane.  A skew field must have been defined in the lens data if any skew points are to be analyzed.  This map includes the extreme points if the number is greater than one, and will include the zero coordinate if the number is odd.

 

If the lens does not have a skew field defined, you can click the button “Define skew field” to set XP0 equal to YP0.  Then you can request a nonzero GBAR field.

 

CRECT

is similar to RECT except that any (fractional) field points lying outside a unit circle are deleted.  If XPP0 equals YPP0, this results in a circular field map.  For best results, the grid numbers should be odd.

 

CIRCLE

samples a circle of points around the periphery of the field.

 

POINT

samples a single field point at the entered coordinates.  This defines the single field point when mapping over the pupil.

 

DIAMOND

This option crops the RECT grid to points that lie on a diamond whose vertices are at the top, bottom, and both sides.  It is intended for the common case where the field is square or rectangular and you have defined the full-field point (YP0) to equal the distance from the axis to the corner point.  That scheme lets you control the aberrations over the field while doing all of your design and analysis in the meridional plane – which is more convenient than modeling the actual field shape.  The field will be mapped showing a square field as a square, tipped at 45 degrees.  A rectangular field is shown as a tipped square in exploded view, and as a tilted rectangle in actual view.

 

CUSTOM

This entry lets you specify your own set of field points.  Follow this line with a set of additional lines, each giving the

 

HBAR GBAR [ ICOL ]

 

of the next point, up to a total of 50 field points.  The default color is the primary color.  This option is not available in the MMA dialog, so you must create an input MACro to use it.

 

 

The ray grid defined by RGRID is used for mapping over the PUPIL and also defines the rayset used for mapping SPT, PUPIL, and TRANS over the FOV, and the single ray required when mapping most items over the FOV.  It is defined by the RGRID input, shown above.

 

CRECT

traces a square grid of rays filling the pupil, with a grid number of NB.  The grid is masked to a unit circle unless the lens has been declared RPUPIL.  Turning on switch 90 gives a hexapolar grid instead of a square grid.

 

RECT

traces a rectangular grid grid, as PUP 2 in TRACE.  This option works even if the lens has been declared CPUPIL.  The grid numbers in X and Y can be different.  Switch 90 does not apply to this choice.

 

MCIRCLE

traces the maximum circle of NB rays that will get through the system, expanding or contracting each ray as necessary  This is similar to PUP 3 in TRACE.

 

CIRCLE

gives a circle of NB rays around the outside of the pupil.  The circle will contract as necessary to clear all sources of vignetting, but will not expand.  This is similar to PUP 4 in TRACE.  The circle will be a rectangle if RPUPIL is in effect.

 

ICRCIRCLE

 

 

is similar to MCIRCLE, except that the center ray is at the ICR point  rather than at the center of the pupil.  This is useful in cases where the latter ray will not trace.

 

POINT

gives a single ray at the entered fractional pupil coordinates.  This defines the ray when mapping over the field of view (FOV).

 

 

Other input options are as follows:

 

[COLOR { ICOL / P / M} ]

 

 

defines the color number of the rays. Default is the primary color. P is the primary color, and M produces a multicolor analysis over all defined wavelengths

 

[ GIHT NB ]

gives a desired image height with which to compare the ray coordinates for HDIST requests. The default is the paraxial value.

 

[ SCALE TSCF ]

gives a desired mapping scale factor for plots.  The default is unity scale. Thus, if the TSCF is 100, the locations are magnified by 100x.  This refers to the location of each pattern, whereas the size of the pattern is controlled by the analog scale, described below.

 

For example, if you ask for a TSCF of 10.0, then one inch on the plot equals 2.54 mm or 0.1 inch, while a TSCF of 1.0 would equal 25.4 mm or one inch.

 

[ ESCALE REF ]

gives the inverse of the above.  The REF dimension is that dimension, in lens units, that shows up as 1 inch on the plot.

 

At a REF of 10, if the lens is in MM, one inch on the plot would equal 0.3937 in., or 10 mm.

 

[ SCALE AUTO ]

If this form is entered, the program will automatically select a scale that fits the first fiducial within a square of about 4.8 inches on a side.  Since this point is usually at the corner of the array, the other points then usually fit as well.  However, this may not always be the case, and if any of the fiducials are too far from the center of the picture, or out of the field entirely, you can enter an explicit SCALE yourself.

 

ANALOG ASCF

gives the scale factor for the individual diagrams to be drawn at each fiducial point.  Thus an ASCF of 100 would draw the patterns at 100x scale.

 

The CIRCLE option applies to all forms that return a single number; if selected, the data are drawn as a circle whose radius equals that value.  If not selected, the data are plotted as a line.

 

The CSCALE option draws a colored box at the fiducial location, scaled from blue (smallest value) to red (largest).  This option works best for large grid numbers.

 

EANALOG REF

This form gives the reference dimension of the pattern that will be scale to 1 inch on the plot.

 

ANALOG AUTO

causes the program to automatically scale the diagrams so that they fit on the picture.  This adjusts the first diagram displayed (normally near the corners of the array) so the size is about 1 inch.  If the number of fiducials is large, the patterns may overlap at this resulting scale, however, and you may then want to repeat the analysis with a larger EANALOG scale.

 

[ ACTUAL / EXPLODED ]

 

 

these determine whether the position of each data map point (the fiducial) is given by the actual coordinates of the ray at the surface, or is given by the distribution specified in the FGRID or RGRID requests (EXPLODED). For requests without a surface number entered, the default is EXPLODED.

 

Be aware that there are three different rules that determine where the program places the individual fiducials on the page, when ACTUAL is requested:

 

1.  For FOCAL lenses, the location is a function of the geometric chief-ray position.  Distortion is defined as a departure from a fixed ratio between the ray position and the field point.  In this case the mapping is a function of the tangents of the ray angles.

 

2.  For AFOCAL systems, the fiducial location is given by the ray angle, not the tangent, and distortion is defined as a departure from a strict proportionality between that angle and the object angle.

 

3.  For eyepiece designs, one does not want to measure distortion in terms of ray angles.  The APERFECT option offers another way to set up this kind of system.  See Section 7.5.1.

 

If the requested MAP is EXPLODED and over the FOV, the program will add scale bars in X and Y to the picture.  The scale marks will be in linear units if the object has been defined with types A, C, F, and L, and in angular units otherwise.

 

[ DIGITAL / ANALOG [ ASCF ] ]

 

 

 

specify whether the value calculated for the requested parameter at the requested sample point is numbered on the plot or drawn as a diagram whose length or direction gives the desired information.  If ANALOG is requested, the vector data are scaled by ASCF with a default of TSCF.

 

 

[ KEEP ]

causes the program to store the array of mapped data.  Valid only for single-color analysis.

 

[ DIFFERENCES ]

causes the previous data (stored with KEEP) to be subtracted from the current data before the latter are printed or plotted.  Valid only for single-color analysis.

 

[SLANT NB]

requests that the plotted data be arranged on the page in slanted rows, so that the data vectors do not mask each other. NB is the tangent of the slant angle.  Applies to EXPLODED views only.  The analog CIRCLE option gives another way to avoid overlapping lines.

 

[ REFHBAR HBAR ]

specifies the fractional field point to be used as a reference by the DIST analysis, described above.

 

BINOCULAR ...

only applies to a map of spot diagrams over the field.  The color of the spot diagrams is taken from word 2, where LEFT will cause them to be drawn in blue, and RIGHT in red.  The purpose is to enable you to superimpose two maps, each showing what you would see with one eye located at the appropriate pupil position, and see how things line up.  To combine the two maps, you will have to use the CPLOT feature.

 

This feature only makes sense if you select the option to map actual positions, not exploded.

 

PRINT [ FULL ]

 

gives a printed tabulation of data instead of a plot.  The calculated data values are printed either in the form of an array of the dimensions given by the sample geometry, or in a list (FULL) where at each sample point the (X,Y) coordinates and the calculated data value are printed.

 

PLOT

specifies plotted output.

 

GO

gives neither printed nor plotted output.  This is intended when a large number of points are to be saved with the KEEP command, and the next run will obtain the desired results with DIFFERENCES.

 

 

A special form of input can map the surface sag over a grid of points in the surface.  The input is

 

 MAP SAG OVER SURFACE ON SURFACE NB.

 

This form does not involve raytracing, and the grid is defined relative to the clear aperture size (in X and Y) at the surface, rather than at the intersection points of a grid of rays as is the case with the OVER PUPIL and OVER FOV options.  The choices EXPLODED, PRINT, etc. are all available for this form as well.  The form GSAG also calculates the surface sag, but converts the (X,Y,Z) coordinates of each point to the coordinates of surface 1 before listing the output.  The output is therefore in global coordinates and is useful for modeling an aspheric surface located at an arbitrary position within the system.  If the form PRINT FULL is selected, a table of the global coordinates will be printed and can be captured and even used as input for a CAD program for modeling the system.
