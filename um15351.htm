15.3.5.1 Rating potential matches                                                Previous Next TOC

 

XSYS assumes that if the match between the current lens and an example lens (as defined below) is perfect, a portion (or all) of the example, suitably scaled and positioned, could be substituted for a portion (or all) of the starting lens and the result would be a perfect, well-corrected lens.  In practice the match is seldom perfect, and subsequent optimization is usually required.

 

The program evaluates the match between the starting lens, if any, and each suitable example lens at each airspace, comparing the aberrations and overall characteristics of the two.  The lenses are both locally normalized, and the local pupils and fields aligned.  The example is then adjusted in local F/number to match the current lens, and scaled about the local focal plane to optimize the match as determined by the various weights.  The effects on the OPD in each color at each field point of these adjustments are estimated based on a polynomial expansion of the OPD'S.  All combinations are investigated, with the examples taken both forward and backward.  Substitutions are considered where the example portions go either before or behind the current lens portions.  The combinations are then rated as to the closeness of the match.

 

The weights WYB and WUB refer to discrepancies in the chief ray position and angle at the point where a portion of an example lens might be substituted for a portion of the current lens.  The defaults are 1.0 for both, and are probably best left alone. WPUP refers to the equality of spherical aberration of the pupil at the local pupil.  If pupil aberrations have a strong bearing on the aberration correction in a given case, this weight may be increased from its default value of 1.0.  (It refers only to the local mismatch, not the aberration value itself.) WFOV weights the mismatch between the derived example object FOV and the required FOV, when the example is used in modes 3 and 4 (see below).  The default is 10, and a lower value might produce lenses with ray failures.

 

ROOM specifies the importance of avoiding negative airspaces, and the default is 10.  This tends to avoid combinations with a negative thickness between the portions, but it is not necessarily an essential precaution; it is conceivable that an excellent lens could be found where this thickness was slightly negative, and that the problem would be quickly corrected by the optimization program.  You may wish to experiment with a reduced value of ROOM.
