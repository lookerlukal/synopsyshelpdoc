8.6.3.4 Fourier-transform diffraction MTF                 Next Previous TOC

 

In cases where vector diffraction plays a role, or if the effects of polarization are to be considered (the lens must be in polarization mode – see chapter 17), the diffraction modulation transfer function may be obtained from dialogs MOP or MFM, or with the input

 

[W]DMTF { ICOL / M / P } HBAR NRYS 0 GBAR [ P ]

 

where

 

ICOL

is the color number, “M” for multicolor, or “P” for the primary color.

 

HBAR

is the fractional Y-object height

 

NRYS

is the desired number of rays in the entrance pupil

 

GBAR

is the fractional X-object height

 

[ P ]

indicates that the MTF data should be plotted.

 

The DMTF is calculated by taking the Fourier transform of the line-spread function derived from the point-spread function described in section 8.6.2.  Although more rigorous than the convolution method described in the preceding sections, this method has its own penalties: it is impossible to sample the entire image, which extends to infinity.  It is therefore necessary to compromise between accuracy and cost; the image is sampled over an area about 6 times the radius of the Airy disk, and twice this area if the wide option is selected (WDMTF).  To sample further than this region would increase the cost of the computation, while the amount of additional energy that would be accounted for would steadily decrease as one sampled yet further.  The current sampling algorithm is a compromise.  The residual error in the MTF analysis is in most cases less than one percent at the lowest spatial frequencies, and even less at higher frequencies, for an image corrected to better than a wave of aberration.

 

The wide option calculates the MTF in X and Y at 64 spatial frequencies, and at + and – 45 degrees at 50 frequencies. The narrow option computes the MTF in X and Y at 21 spatial frequencies.  The program selects as a default cutoff the theoretical cutoff for a lens of that F/number (calculated as in section 8.6), at the primary wavelength, or the color specified if monochromatic.  If the transmitted portion of the pupil is much smaller than the size of YMP1, you may wish to reduce the size of YMP1 so that the F/number and the cutoff frequency will correspond more nearly to that of the physical image.

 

If a cutoff other than the default is desired, the FCO input rnay also be used, as in the case of the GMTF, but only for the wide option,  This will plot only those values that are at or below the requested FCO.

 

If your current FCO is significantly below the diffraction cutoff, you will get a smoother MTF plot with the wide option, since it calculates more MTF values and there are then more data points in the reduced data range.

 

If the exit pupil is strongly foreshortened in the Y-direction, the image dimension in the X-direction will be much smaller than in Y.  There may then be too few samples in X to get an accurate MTF.  The wide option (WDMTF) may then be more accurate, since it uses a finer grid of samples.

 

For reference, a perfect DMTF is also plotted at the first wavelength analyzed, on DMTF plots.  This reference ignores apodization and vignetting, unlike the perfect MTF given with the convolution methods, which model the actual pupil.

You can also get a display of a 3-dimensional MTF surface with the dialog MIF, and 2-dimensional contours of the DMTF with the input

 

M2F { ICOL / M / P } HBAR NRYS 0 GBAR.

 

This feature always employs wide-area sampling and plots out to the natural cutoff frequency, ignoring FCO input, if any.  Bear in mind that in order to avoid aliasing error, you must request an adequate number of rays.  See MIF for a discussion of this topic.
