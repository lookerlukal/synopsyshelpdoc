5.33 V-number calculation (VNUM)                Previous Next TOC

 

For correcting chromatic aberration one must have a selection of materials with differing dispersions.  It is easy to find such materials in the glass catalogs or displayed on the screen as described in Section 13.3.5.  But working in the infrared presents a problem since there are fewer materials and because the effective Abbe number (V-number) changes from one spectral region to another.  Germanium, for example, behaves like a crown glass in the 8-12 uM band but shows the larger dispersion of a flint glass in the 3-5 uM region.  For any spectral region where one has three indices at three wavelengths, one can define the V-number of a material by

 

VNUM = (N2 - 1)/(N3 - N1).

 

The command sequence

 

VNUM [ WAV1 WAV2 WAV3 ]

 

{ S / O / H / F/ U / C / G / M } glassname

…

END

 

Will give a listing of the effective V-number of all listed materials at the entered wavelength range.  The glass catalog is specified with { S / O / H / F/ U / C / G } as with GTB lens input.  You may input the wavelengths at which to calculate the results; the default is the wavelengths currently in the lens.

 

Here is how to obtain a listing in the 3-5 uM region for a list of materials:

 

VNUM 5 4 3

U CAFL

U GE

U AMTIR1

U ZNS

U AS2S3

END

 

The results are shown below

 

EFFECTIVE V-NUMBER OVER WAVELENGTH RANGE   5.000000  4.000000  3.000000

 

CAFL     1.409640  21.68301

GE       4.024904 103.60717

AMTIR1   2.514015 179.51608

ZNS      2.251782 109.79658

AS2S3    2.411143 156.67098

 

Each line gives the index at the center wavelength and the effective V-number.  Here we see that CAFL has a high dispersion, while that of AMTIR1 is much lower.  These would be good candidates for chromatic correction in this band.  A warning message will be displayed if the entered wavelengths fall outside the valid range of any of the selected materials.

 

Note that this input, like all input involving glass names, must be typed as shown above, on separate lines.  This means that you cannot concatenate more than one line with the “/” character, as you can with most SYNOPSYS input.
