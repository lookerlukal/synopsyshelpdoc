5.13 Buried Ghost Image (BGI)                        Next Previous TOC

 

Four features can analyze the properties of a ghost image that is formed by a single reflection within a lens system.  These are

 

BGI        paraxial buried-ghost image analysis

RGI        real-ray analysis of a specified buried-ghost image

FGI        to calculate the fractional input power at various zones at a ghost image

GHPLOT        can show the ray paths of a buried ghost

 

These features can be run from the dialog MGH.

 

(To analyze ghost images at the final surface formed by two reflections from lens surfaces, the GHOST command is used.)

 

In a high-power laser system it is sometimes possible to obtain dangerous energy densities as a result of unwanted reflections from lens surfaces, if these result in a low F/number ghost image at some sensitive point in the system.

 

The ordinary paraxial raytrace and ghost image programs consider the image geometry and ghost location after the beam has passed through the entire system, and ignore the beam geometry inside the lens.  If you need to monitor ghost beam dimensions inside the lens, the command

 

BGI [ RADIUS ]

 

gives, for each refracting surface in the system, the location of all intermediate ghost images, the F/number of the beam at the ghost image, and the semi-aperture of the beam at the surfaces on either side of the ghost.  If the optional RADIUS is entered, only those ghosts smaller than that radius are shown.  A typical output line might read

 

10            3 -  4          -4.2520        -0.3609         2.3508

 

which says that the beam reflected from surface 10 forms an intermediate image between surfaces 3 and 4, the local (paraxial) speed is F/4.252, and the beam radius is -0.3609 on surface 3 and 2.3508 on surface 4.

You can also control the ghost image radius at any surface, as explained in Section 10.3.1.5.

 

Since a paraxial buried ghost can appear to have a radius that is smaller than the diffraction disk at that F/number, it is useful to add a correction, equal to the primary wavelength times the F/number.  Turn on switch 49 to activate this feature.

 

See Section 18.3.6 for an example of how to evaluate a buried ghost in nonsequential mode.

 

 

Real-Ray Buried Ghosts

 

The BGI command produces a paraxial analysis of buried ghost images, but you can also evaluate the properties of a particular ghost in more detail, with real rays, with the command

 

RGI JREFL JHOT XEN YEN [ SURF ]                (Real-ray Ghost Image)

 

JREFL is the reflecting surface, JHOT is the surface near the ghost where the beam size is to be monitored to detect possible overheating from the input laser, and XEN and YEN are the fractional pupil position of the ray that you want to trace.  The optional SURF produces the ray path in and back to JHOT.  To evaluate the ghost in the above table on surface 3 from a reflection from surface 10 at the marginal ray, you would enter

 

RGI 10 3 0 1

 

You can use this feature to plot the intercept coordinate as a function of the entrance coodinate (like a rayfan curve, but at the intermediate ghost image) with the AI looping capability.  For instance, you could create a MACro that reads

 

RGI 10 3 0 AIP

ORD = FILE 2

 

Run this MACro once, and then type the AI sentence “DO MACRO FOR AIP = 0 TO 1”.  The desired curve is then plotted.

 

Be aware that if you select the SURF option, the Z-axis sign convention is reversed when the ray reaches the reflecting surface.  The program actually constructs a reversed version of the entire lens (which you cannot see), and traces (forward) from there to JHOT, to complete the raytrace.  As a result, the quantities ZZ, HH, and Z are opposite to the usual sign for that portion of the analysis.

 

Fractional-Energy Buried Ghosts

 

Yet another feature can estimate the local power density and fractional input power in several zones at the ghost image.  The input is

 

 FGI JREFL JHOT RADIUS [ POWER ]

 

The program divides surface JHOT into 25 circular zones out to a radius of RADIUS, and traces 1000 rays evenly spaced in the meridional plane of the entrance pupil.  The energy at each pupil point is found from the Gaussian intensity distribution of the input beam (which must be defined with the OBG object form for this feature).  The fraction of the total input energy in each zone is then recorded and tabulated, along with the energy density in the zone.  (Diffraction is ignored in this feature.)

 

Let us illustrate.  Suppose that you send a pulsed laser beam with a power of 50.0e-6 Joules into a lens system, and want to ensure that the buried-ghost image flux remains below 0.001 Joules/mm**2.  It is useful to calculate the waist size that would just yield this power density at the center of the beam.  This is given by

 

 W**2 = 2*ETOTAL/PI*EPEAK

 

Here W is the beam waist, of peak power density EPEAK, and the total beam power is ETOTAL.  If ETOTAL = 50.0e-6 Joules and EPEAK = 0.001Joules/mm**2, this equation gives W = 0.1784 mm.  If the OBG in this system specified a relative beam size of 2.0, which is often done to avoid vignetting the gaussian beam, then you want to look for any place where the BGI beam is smaller than 0.356.

 

Assume that the BGI listing showed a potential problem with a reflection at surface 35 and a ghost on surface 32.

 

SYNOPSYS AI>BGI .356

BURIED GHOST IMAGE ANALYSIS

 

REFL SURF   GHOST LOCATION      F/NUMBER           YA1           YA2

____________________________________________________________________

   35           31 - 32          10.4216         0.4253        -0.0300

 

Here the ghost on surface 32 has a radius of only -0.0300 mm – smaller than the allowed value of W.  Let us see how big the blur is when real rays are traced:

From the above table we see that the peak energy density is 0.140 Joules/mm**2, which is about 140 times greater than our limit.  Thus we must ensure that surface 35 has an antireflection coating of about 1/140, or 0.00714 reflectivity, in order to avoid damage to surface 32.

 

This feature assumes circular symmetry in the ghost image, ignores diffraction, vignetting, and reflection losses in the beam, and traces from the on-axis object.
