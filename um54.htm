5.4 Narcissus                                        Next Previous TOC

 

The command

 

NARCISSUS

 

gives a listing of the value of the quantity ΦAoN at each surface.  The elements are defined below.

 

 

When designing a scanning infrared system with a cooled detector, it is important to insure that no surface has the property of reflecting cold radiation originating at the detector back onto the detector.  If this situation occurs, the geometry that makes such retroreflection possible can only occur near the center of the scan pattern, and the somewhat lower total flux received by the detector at that instant will cause a "cold spike", or spurious signal change, which appears as a dark smudge at the center of the display.

 

It is possible to determine, by means of quantities calculated during a paraxial raytrace of the lens, the amount by which the reflected radiation from a given surface is defocused when it reaches the detector.  If it is quite out of focus, the energy density arising from the retroreflection will be small and the narcissus effect from that surface will not be significant.

 

The path of a retroreflected ray is shown in the above figure, and the parameters Ao and Φ are identified.  The quantity N is the index of refraction of the space to the right of the surface.  Given this geometry, the blur size back at the detector can be shown to be (in the limit of small Φ)

 

 Y1 = 2 Φ Ao N / α

 

where α is the final convergence half angle of the beam at the detector, and Y1 is the half-height of the blur.  The intensity of the cold spike is inversely proportional to Y1**2, so the listing of ΦAoN indicates which surfaces are serious narcissus sources.  For AFOCAL systems the (half) angular defocus of the beam is 2ΦAoN/af where af is the half aperture of the normal exiting beam.

 

The narcissus contribution of an individual surface can be controlled in the merit function. See Section 10.3.3.  You can also analyze the geometric properties of the narcissus with GHPLOT.

 

A sample NARCISSUS output is shown below.  The calculation is done twice, first for the paraxial marginal ray, giving YNI, and then for the chief ray, giving CYNI.  In the event that the chief ray intercept angle is very small for a given point in the field at that surface, then that chief ray will likely return to the detector, and the narcissus, even if severe, will be relatively constant over the field up to there.  So, while a small value of YNI is to be avoided, since it leads to a pronounced narcissus effect, a small value of CYNI is to be desired, since the effect is less noticeable.  The ratio M/C is therefore a useful way to judge whether the narcissus from a given surface will be objectionable.  The FNAR command, described below provides a more complete evaluation of the narcissus as a function of field angle.

In some systems, the region that is physically capable of originating cold radiation is significantly larger than the detector area itself.  In such systems, the narcissus may be limited by vignetting of the retroreflected beam at some intermediate surface, called the "cold stop', and not by virtue of the beam overfilling the detector, which the previous calculation assumes.

 

The beam responsible for narcissus flux from a given surface must pass backward through the system from the detector, undergo reflection, and then propagate forward until it intersects the cooled surface.  Any portion of the beam that is vignetted by a warm surface will not contribute to the narcissus.  The cold stop is that surface beyond which any forward-traveling ray must eventually hit a cold surface.  It may be designated by the input.

 

NAR SN [ AP ]

 

where SN gives the surface number and AP the clear aperture radius.  The default aperture is that given on the CAP listing.  If this input is given, the narcissus calculation described above will be followed by a table giving the retroreflected beam aperture at the cold stop due to each surface preceding it, and the ratio of the cold stop area to that of the beam.  Paraxial ray heights determine the size of the initial beam, and paraxial raytracing is performed.  An example is shown in below.

NARCISSUS OVER FIELD

 

While the previous section describes features that analyze paraxial rays, it is also possible to evaluate the narcissus with real rays.  This feature is FNAR, and the input is

 

 

FNAR

 

NRYS nrys

 

ICOL icol

 

JNAR jn1 jn2 ... jn10

 

NSTEPS nsteps

 

REFL refl

 

CLOC cloc cao

 

DISPLAY background white black

 

END

 

 

In this input,

 

nrys is the number of rays to be traced in a grid

 

icol is the color number

 

jn1 to jn10 are the surfaces whose narcissus is to be evaluated

 

nsteps is the number of locations in the field to be analyzed

 

refl is the reflection coefficient of surface jnar

 

cloc is the location of the cold stop, relative to surface 1, and cao is the clear aperture radius of that stop

 

If DISPLAY is entered, the program will create a greyscale bitmap showing the narcissus one would see when the display is adjusted so the background is at level background, the white value is where the display is saturated and looks white, and the black value is displayed as black.  Those entries account for the offset and gain of the display electronics.  An example is given below.

 

 

To use this feature, surface number 1 should be the entrance pupil of the lens.  Consider the following lens:

 

This is a FLIR telescope, arranged in reverse.  Radiation actually comes in from the right, but here we want to examine narcissus rays, not those from the target.

 

We assume that there is a detector of some kind off the page to the left, and the incoming rays shown all are routed there by means of a scanning prism or some other mechanism, not shown.  The rays depicted are actually coming from the right, but we want to trace those rays backwards, to see what else the detector is looking at.  There is a declared cold stop located at a distance cloc to the left of surface 1, with semi aperture cao.  This is modeled by shifting the location of the cold stop and decentering the CAO to follow the direction the sensor is looking (since the scanning prisms are not modeled.)

 

If the rays we are tracing backwards (to the right) reflect from a surface somewhere and return to the left, they either get through the cold stop or they do not.  If they get through, it means that the detector, looking at that field angle, sees a reflection of something cold, and the signal at that field point is therefore slightly reduced -- which gives rise to the narcissus seen on the display at that field point.  If those rays do not get through, then the detector sees a reflection of something warm, which does not contribute to the narcissus effect. FNAR can help evaluate the situation in either case.

Here we see that the narcissus from 4 and 5 extends partway over the field and goes to zero thereafter.  The plot for surface 6 shows a gradual slope that extends over the whole field, which would be less noticeable to the eye.  That surface had a higher M/C value than the others, which predicts a less serious narcissus there, which is what we see.

 

Let us now look at the actual rays contributing to the narcissus from surface 4, at field point 0.2, which showed none of the rays getting through on the plot above.  For this we turn off VIG mode (so the vignetted rays are not deleted), and use GHPLOT  to show the ray paths for a fan of rays.

 

GHPLOT 55 P 1 .2 0  4  R

R 0.01 ALL

BURIED 4 1

DRAD 0.01

SIMAGE CAO

PER 0 0 0 1 200

PLOT

                                                                                                

 

 

Here we look along the rays in red, going to the right, from the field point 0.2.  Those rays reflect from surface 4 and head to the left, shown in blue -- but they are headed for a point outside the cold stop, which is to the left of surface 1 and decentered downwards in this case, and that is why the trace above falls off as it does.  Those rays are vignetted.

 

By contrast, the return from surface 6, which had a more gradual curve, shows most of the rays returning through the cold stop, even from the edge of the field, and that is why the curve was less steep in that case.

 

 

DISPLAY EMULATION

 

 

FNAR can also show a simulation of the display of a FLIR system with one or more surfaces contributing to the narcissus effect.  Here we show the effect when four surfaces are selected:

 

CHG

VIG

END

FNAR

NRYS 1000

ICOL 2

JNAR 4 5 6 7

NSTEPS 50

REFL 0.01

CLOC -50 5

DISPLAY .8 .78 .82

END

 

This will model the display when it is adjusted so the background level is at 0.8, on a scale where 0 is black and 1 is white.  The narcissus contributions will lower that value at affected field points, and the gain is adjusted so that a level of 0.78 is displayed as black and 0.82 as white.  Here is the resulting plot, which shows a conspicuous narcissus.

 

 

I have to comment on this lens, which was obtained from the patent literature.  The narcissus effect is not well corrected, and if I had designed the lens it would be much better!

 

The YNI value on surface 5, 0.2625, is above the range where one might expect a serious narcissus -- but that depends on the nature of the dewar geometry.  If the detector is at the end of a "cold finger" of small dimensions and everything around it is warm, the narcissus flux is much lower than if a sizable cold stop is followed by a dewar where everything inside is at cryogenic temperatures.  In that case, which is the case modeled above, the value of YNI is not a reliable measure of the narcissus.  Instead, one wants a large value of M/C, as listed on the NAR output.  This quantity can be controlled in optimization with the operand RNAR.
