10.3.1.5 Ghost-image control                                                Next Previous TOC

 

A ghost image is caused by a reflection from one or more refracting surfaces.  SYNOPSYS can evaluate and control two types: The GHOST program can show which combinations of surfaces are responsible for ghost images at the image surface, and BGI can evaluate the properties of a ghost image that is formed at another place within a lens system.

 

To control the size of the blur at the image from a selected paraxial ghost, the input is

 

M TAR WT A PGHOST JREFH JREFL

 

M TAR WT A PUGHOST JREFH JREFL

 

The first of these, PGHOST, is used to control the blur size of a paraxial ghost of a point in object space.  This is the usual ghost image situation, where a bright object in one part of the field forms a ghost image in another part.  The second variety, PUGHOST, is used to control the size of the ghost image of a point on the entrance pupil.

 

Suppose that the ghost resulting from a reflection from surface 15 and then from surface 9 is sharply focused at the image plane.  A small ghost is a bright ghost, and you can reduce its intensity by increasing the blur size.  Suppose that you want the ghost to have a blur radius of not less than 5 mm.  Here is how you could target it to exactly this value in the AANT file input:

 

M 5 1 A PGHOST 15 9

 

Since a larger ghost is not a defect, it is often better to give a one-sided aberration:

 

LL 5 .1 4 1

A PGHOST 15 9

 

Here the defect has a value of 0.1 if the ghost radius is 5.0, and a value of 1.0 if the radius drops to 4.0.  Note that the returned value of the aberration is always positive, and the target value should therefore be positive as well.

 

Do not turn on switch 47 if you use this feature for optimization.  That option is intended for detailed analysis of a single ghost, and it will produce a great deal of unwanted printout if this switch setting is used in optimization.

 

Like the GHOST command, this feature will augment the calculated paraxial ghost radius by the estimated diffraction disk radius if switch 49 is on (but not for PUGHOST).

 

You can also control the size of a ghost that is formed within a lens system (a “buried ghost”).  This is useful in high-power laser systems, where a ghost reflection from a lens surface can cause damage to the elements if sharply focused.  The input for this kind of control is

 

M TAR WT A BGI JREFL JHOT

 

where JREFL is the surface that reflects the light, and JHOT is the surface where you want to monitor the flux to prevent damage.  The LL form is also recommend for this kind of control.  Thus, to ensure that the beam radius of the ghost reflected from surface 41 on surface 9 remains larger than 0.2 units, you could input

 

LL 0.2 .01 0.1 5 A BGI 41 9

 

in the merit function.  This example gives a penalty of 0.01 if the ghost radius is 0.2, and a penalty of 5 if it shrinks to 1.5.  Note that the control is applied to the absolute value of the blur radius, so the program will let it assume a large positive or negative value, as the situation requires.

 

Also note that both the PGH and BGI controls are paraxial quantities, and do not involve real raytracing.  They therefore ignore apertures, tilts, and decenters.  Both will honor switch 49 and add the estimated diffraction disk radius to the calculated geometric ghost image radius if this switch is on.
