3.3.2.4 Holographic optical elements (HOE's)       Previous Next TOC

 

(These options go into an RLE or CHG file, and may be entered interactively with the dialog SPS.)

 

A hologram constructed by exposing an emulsion to two coherent beams can be defined as an optical element to SYNOPSYS.  The HOE can be either refractive or reflective, and the substrate may be of any shape permitted by the normal surface options.  In addition, the beams used in constructing the HOE may contain aberrations defined explicitly by the user or implicitly by means of auxiliary optical systems.  Most of the parameters used to define the HOE or the auxiliary systems may also be entered either as variables or aberrations in the optimization program.

 

A HOE requires at least two surfaces just to describe the properties of the reference beams and emulsion. For transmission HOEs a third surface is usually required to define the other side of the substrate.

The rules for entering HOE's are as follows:

 

1.

Two surfaces are used to describe the HOE shape and emulsion. The first of the two is given the index of the emulsion as seen on playback.  No curvature or thickness option is required for this surface, and it must be declared a HOE by the input SN HOE.  The substrate shape is given on the second of the two HOE surfaces, and the index assigned to this surface represents that of the substrate (usually some kind of glass).  A coating may be assigned to the substrate surface.

 

2.

The index and index modulation of the emulsion during exposure are given by the HIN input.

 

3.

The construction wavelength is given by the CWAV input.

 

4.

The emulsion thickness is given by the HTH input.  This and the index modulation are used to estimate the diffraction efficiency, with an enhanced version of the method of Kogelnik (H. Kogelnik, Bell Sys. Tech. J., 48, No.9, (November, 1969), pp.2909-2945).  Only single-mode diffraction is considered, but off-Bragg incidence is included.  (In polarization mode both the S- and P-plane efficiencies are computed; otherwise only the S-plane is given.)

 

5.

The two coherent beams are defined by the (X,Y, Z) coordinates of two point objects with the input P1 and P2.  The X, Y, and Z coordinates have their usual meaning, in the local coordinate system of the HOE; the Z coordinate is positive if the point is to the right of the HOE.  The (X,Y,Z) coordinates are not sufficient themselves to uniquely define the phase differences of the two beams at each point on the HOE, however, since each point may be either a real or virtual image.  To remove ambiguity, an additional phase factor, PHA, is required for each point.  It may be interpreted as follows:

 

For a transmission HOE, the two points will be illuminated from the same side of the HOE.  If the actual position of the point P1 or P2 is on the left of the HOE (i.e., in the –Z direction) the phase factor PHA is +1.  If the point is on the right of the HOE the factor is -1.  If either point is not in AIR, the immersion index may be entered in word 6.

 

For a reflection HOE, the two points are illuminated from opposite sides, and the rule depends on the diffraction order chosen.  If ORD -1 is input, point P1 will be considered illuminated from the left, and the rule for PHA is the same as above.  The point P2 is illuminated from the right of the HOE, and the rule is reversed: PHA is equal to +1 if the point P2 is to the right.  If the ORD is +1, then point P1 uses the reverse rule and P2 the normal rule.

 

6.

The two beams are normally considered perfect point sources, but aberrations may be defined in the form of a power-series polynomial with the entries A11, A12, etc., or a Zernike polynomial using the entries B11, etc.  The G-values give the OPD error of the construction beam as a function of the (X,Y) coordinates of a point on the HOE using the polynomial representation given in section 9.4, and the B-values the error with the expansion listed in section 3.3.2.2.6.

 

There is a significant difference between these expansions and the forms applied to image interpolation, however.  In the case of HOE's, the coordinates (X,Y) are actual coordinates at the HOE in lens units rather than normalized coordinates at a pupil.  Thus, to cause the beam P1 to have an OPD error varying as the fourth power of the radius and equal to 1.5 waves at a radius of 1.0 units, the input might be

 

A11 0 1.5

 

The G-terms can be varied in optimization, but you have to use the addressing scheme explained in Section 10.2.  Zernike terms are varied differently, with RHG and LHG variables.

 

The coefficients are normally entered in OPD units, but you may also enter them in lens units.  To do so, include the entry WLENS before you enter the coefficients themselves.  In this case, the coefficients refer to the phase shift in lens units. WOPD will restore the default.

 

7.

An auxiliary optical system or systems may be defined as the source of the aberrations instead of explicit terms entered as in the above paragraph.  There are no restrictions on the size or nature of these auxiliary systems, and they are really completely separate lenses that must be defined by the user prior to creating the system containing the HOE.  Normally you will set up and store them in the lens library, as needed.  The only requirements on these systems are that the next-to-last surface correspond geometrically in size, shape, and position to the HOE substrate, and that the full-field image point correspond to the point P1 or P2.  In other words, these systems should correspond exactly to the geometry that will be employed in making the HOE, where the substrate is a dummy surface preceding the image, and the full-field image (both HBAR and GBAR equal to 1.0) is at the reference point P1 or P2.  Since the OPD function is to be taken at the HOE surface, and not at infinity as is usually the case when tracing rays, you should turn off the Debye calculation for the auxiliary systems by turning on switch 66.

 

8

EFILE edges cannot be assigned to a HOE.

 

To use an auxiliary system and transfer its data to the HOE, get that system from the library and then enter the command

 
       HP1 [-1]        Hologram Point 1
or        HP2 [-1] .        2
 
to use the power-series polynomial, and
 
       HZ1 [-1]
or        HZ2 [-1]

 

to use the Zernike representation.  The program indicates the accuracy of the curve fit by printing the maximum and RMS OPD difference between the exact data found over a grid of rays and the interpolated data from the expansion.  If switch 13 is ON, the error at each sample ray will also be displayed.  If these errors are a small fraction of a wave, the expansion may be used with confidence; if the power-series expansion is not adequate, the more lengthy Zernike series may be tried.

 

The program will calculate the values of the coefficients from a real raytrace of the system, and store the values in a temporary file along with the image coordinates.  After this step has been performed, the system containing the HOE is created or retrieved from the library.  A CAO should be inserted on the HOE itself, by the user; the program verifies that the construction clear aperture is at least as large as that of the HOE.  Then, in a CHG file, the HOE surface is declared with the HOE input, as above.  Instead of introducing coefficients with the input A11 etc., the entry

 

       GA1        Get Aberrations 1

or        GA2        Get Aberrations 2

 

is input, causing the program to fetch the stored coefficients and to assign them to the points P1 or P2.  The image location in X, Y, and Z is also retrieved and assigned to the X, Y, Z values for that point.  A warning message will be printed if the major wavelength of the auxiliary system differs from the construction wavelength entered on the CWAV input, or if the clear aperture of the former is less than that of the HOE  (The coefficients are only valid out to the aperture over which they were defined.)  If the optional [-1] appears in word 2, the signs of the coefficients will be reversed before they are transferred to the HOE.

 

8.

The final input to the HOE section of the RLE or CHG file is the ORDER line, which gives the diffraction order.  This input is mandatory, and must be entered each time a SN HOE line is included.  A value of-1 will cause a beam that appears to emanate from point P1 to be diffracted so as to converge to point P2, and a value of +1 will do the reverse.  Note that only one case is physically meaningful at a time.  Order -1 is recommended.

 

9.

If a CAO is desired on the HOE to control vignetting, it may be placed on the second of the two HOE surfaces.  Tilts and decenters, however, go on the HOE surface itself, not the substrate.

 

10.

The rules and input described above all refer to the common case of a thick, volume hologram.  It is also possible to model a reflective surface hologram, with somewhat different rules.  The points P1 and P2 for such a case are defined the same way as for a thick transmission HOE, but the thickness HTH is declared to be zero.  Reflection is indicated by declaring the substrate surface REFLECTIVE.  The efficiency calculation is bypassed in this case, giving an efficiency of 1.0 for that surface.

 

11.

Many features of a HOE may be controlled during optimization, including the local grating frequency, departure from the Bragg condition, and efficiency (see 10.3.1.2). Construction aberrations may be picked up in multiconfiguration optimization (see 10.7.2).

Examples of HOE input

 

1. We want to make a HOE from two perfect coherent points, and to reconstruct the beam. Suppose the illumination system looks like this:

 

 

We will set up a system with the object corresponding to point P1 and expect the image to be formed at point P2.

 

RLE

ID HOLOGRAPHIC ELEMENT

WA1 .6328

OBA 10 1.5 1                ! real object at P1

2 IND 1.525                ! index of emulsion on playback

3 TH .05 GTB S        ! this is the substrate

BK7

3 CAO 1

4 TH -5

4 CAO 1

5                                ! image plane at location P2 (see below)

2 HOE

HIN 1.525 .002        HOE exposure index, modulation

CWAV .6328

HTH .0004

P1 0 1.5 -10 1

P2 0 -1 -5 1

ORDER -1

END

 

Here we have modeled the substrate of the HOE as a glass plate of thickness 0.05 inches.  The image formed by the HOE of the object at HBAR = 1 will fall approximately at point P2 – except for aberrations and defocus caused by the glass plate, which was not modeled in this HOE example.

 

2. In this example points P1 and P2 are physically similar to those in example 1, but the HOE is to be a reflection HOE.

 

RLE

ID REFLECTION HOE

WA1 .6328

OBA 10 1.5 1

2 INDEX 1.48

3 TH -5 AIR

3 REFLECTOR                substrate is declared a reflector

2 HOE

HIN 1.47364 .00014

CWAV .6328

HTH .00025

P1 0 1.5 -10 1                PHA is negative for P2. For an aluminized HOE, it would be +1.

P2 0 -1 -5 -1

ORD -1

4                        the image surface

END

 

Note in this example that the HOE gives a real image on surface 4; in the previous example the image was virtual.

 

3. Here we will create a reflective HOE using an auxiliary system to define the aberrations of one of the reference beams.

 

Consider the system in the sketch below.  The HOE will be on a curved substrate, and both points P1 and P2 will be to the right of the HOE.  Point P2 is at a great distance but on the optical axis, and point P1 is the virtual image formed by a negative lens.

 

 

We will first define the auxiliary system:

 

RLE

ID HOE REFERENCE POINT P1

WA1 .6328

OBB 0.0000001 .125 .1                ! Input beam initially collimated, very small FOV

LRAYS                                ! Object in air, but entering beam goes to the left

1 RD .3456 TH -.075 GTB S        ! first lens surface.  Note negative TH

BK7

2 RD -3.45 TH -9.624 AIR        

3 RD 25 TH 8 AIR                ! this will be the HOE

4                                ! this will be point P1

3 AT -30 0 100                        ! HOE tilt angle

END                                ! Focus the system, so that the full-field image is fairly good

PANT        

VY 3 TH

END

AANT

M 0 1 A P YC 1 0 .1

END

SYNO 5

 

When this system is entered into SYNOPSYS, we do a SPEC and CAP to check the position of the image point and the aperture on surface 3.  These must correspond to the point P1 and the aperture of the final HOE.  Assuming that these quantities are satisfactory, we save the system in the lens library and request that the aberration coefficients be prepared for the HOE system (which we have not yet created).

 

 STORE 5        save the auxiliary system

 HP1                compute the coefficients

 

Now we can make the HOE system:

 

 

RLE

 

ID HOE WITH ABERRATIONS

 

WA1 .6328

! this is the playback wavelength.

OBA -8.89796 5.13724 2.5

! this is the object point.  Since it is near point Pl we would expect the image to form near point P2.

LRAYS

! Light enters going to the left

1 AIR

 

2 CAO 2.5 IND 1.5

! Fix CAO of the HOE and playback index.

3 RD 25 TH 1000

! The image will be here.

3 REFLECTOR

! Will be a reflective HOE; this is the substrate

4

 

2 HOE

 

HIN 1.5 .001        

 

CWAV .6328

! This wavelength must be the same as the major wavelength of the auxiliary system.

HTH .0002

 

P1 0 5 8 -1

! This point will get the aberrations. Approx.  (XYZ) coords. will be replaced with the actual image position, in the coordinate system of surface 3.

P2 0 0 1000 1        

! Point P2 is 1000 inches away.

ORD -1

 

END

 

CHG

 

2 HOE

 

GA1

! This picks up the coefficients we created above.

ORD -1

 

END
