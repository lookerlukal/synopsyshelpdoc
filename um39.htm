3.9 Prism Library                                        Previous Next TOC

 

To help you enter prisms into SYNOPSYS, a variety of basic forms may be specified by merely stating the prism type and giving the width, in lens units, and the index of refraction.  The program sets up all of the required surfaces, using LOCAL positions and tilts to describe the geometry, and UAPs to define the shape of the active surfaces.  For prisms involving a roof surface, the program also defines the required LOOSE and CAPTURE surfaces and places the system in NOSEQUENTIAL mode (see Chapter 18).   The general form of input, which goes in the RLE or CHG file, is

 

 SN PRISM name.

 

The program places a prism flag on the surface which is recognized by the programs PER, RPER, SOLID, and RSOLID – causing them to construct the parts of the prism that are not optically active, but which must appear on the drawings to complete the prism.

 

Each type of prism requires a particular number of surfaces, indicated in the listings below, which you must leave available.  Any user data on those surfaces will be replaced by the prism data when the prism is defined, and after editing the system with PAD or LE.  If it is necessary to alter the data on the various prism surfaces – to change face angles, for example – the prism flag must first be removed by the CHG entry

 

SN NPRISM.

 

Then the prism surfaces become normal surfaces, which you may alter as you wish.

 

Note that prisms are not rendered by the basic lens drawing programs DWG and PAD / D.  Instead, the programs PER, [R]SOLID, and PAD / PP are recommended (see 7.2 and 13.3.1).

 

You are advised not to put a prism on surface 1, because surface 2 (which is defined in local coordinates) is then positioned relative to surface 1.  But in global coordinates it is also defined WRT surface 1, so there is a conflict.  In this case, the program will change surface 2 to global, which works perfectly well – but if you make more changes and are unaware that there is now a global coordinate involved, you can get unexpected results. So start all prisms on surface 2 or higher.

 

By far the easiest way to insert a prism into your system is with the WorkSheet.  All you need to do is allow a space between elements where you want the prism to go, and then click the WS button .  Select your prism from the displayed set, enter a face dimension, and click on that space in the lens drawing.  Your prism will be inserted, and all following surfaces will be adjusted if necessary to account for either positive or negative space following the prism.  If your system is folded on itself it may not be clear just where you clicked in the lens drawing, so use the button instead, and follow the instructions in the dialog.  The prism will initially be assigned glass type BK7, but for most prisms you can change it later if you want another glass type.  (Just assign the desired type to that surface in the WorkSheet.)

 

Another WorkSheet option is used for the common situation where you want to model a prism with a slab of glass in the initial design stages.  Optimization can be faster, and the PAD D drawing is then appropriate for monitoring the design.  (Once a real prism is inserted, the perspective drawing is required for rendering the shape properly.)  But when the design is stable, you may want to change the slab into a real prism in order to show the actual system geometry.  If your system currently contains a dummy slab, then in the Insert Prism dialog, select the option “Replace dummy glass slab with prism”.  Then select the prism you wish to substitute for the slab and enter the width, as usual.  When you then click on the slab in the PAD drawing, it is removed and the prism substituted, with the same index as the slab.

 

The reverse procedure is available in the Remove Prism button.

Most of the prism types can be made of any optical material – but the dove and double dove prisms, whose dimensions vary with index of refraction, comes from the library with a fixed index (glass type BK7) and appropriate dimensions.  No index should be entered in this case.

 

The several prism types are described in the following sections, along with an indication of the coordinate systems in effect in front of and behind the prism.  In many cases, the thicknesses following the prism will have the opposite sign from that before, and this is indicated in the appropriate sections and also in the Command Window when the prism data are entered.  The material before a prism should be AIR, and the same is automatically true following the prism.

 

If you need to know the dimensions of a prism after it is inserted into a system, the commands ASY GLOBAL and ASY LOCAL (see 4.6) will give the positions and angles of each surface, and the CAP listing gives the UAP data defining the positions of the vertices of each face, in lens units (see 3.3.1.1).

 

One last word about prisms:  Many of them involve a roof surface, which requires the lens to be put into nonsequential mode.  The raytrace is then much slower than is sequential mode.  If you want to run the optimization program on a system with a roof prism in it, you will probably get tired of waiting.  I do.  There is a simple solution, however, that works in most cases.  If you turn on switch 37, the program will trace the system as if it were sequential – which is much faster – and will give exactly the same image analysis, and therefore the same optimization results, as long as the merit function does not control ray coordinates at the roof itself.  The only time this will not work is when nonsequential mode is required for reasons other than the presence of a roof prism, such as with the DDOVE.

 

Tolerancing a prism involves considerations not encountered with other surface types.  This is explained in Chapter 12.

 

It is occasionally necessary to remove a prism flag, such as when you want to vary the locations of individual surface, for example.  Suppose the lens has a Schmidt prism at surface 12 with an aperture dimension of 15 mm.  You would enter

 

CHG

12 NPRISM

END

 

to remove the prism flag, perform your analysis, and then restore the flag with

 

CHG

12 PRISM SCHMIDT 15

END
