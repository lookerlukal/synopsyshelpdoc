17.2.4 Reflective coatings                                                        Next Previous TOC

 

During polarization raytracing, the energy content of a ray is monitored at each surface, and if, for example, a reflection occurs at a surface in air with no coating specified, the program will assume that the surface is an uncoated interface with a medium of index 1.5 – giving about 4% reflectance.  It is therefore necessary, if polarization raytracing is to be performed, that coatings are accurately modeled on all reflective surfaces.  (Transmissive surfaces without coatings are modeled as a stack with indices of 1.0 for air and the substrate index.)

 

Reflective coatings will often involve a substrate with a complex index.  Whether these are standard or custom, the program will examine the stack to determine if it can be used intact or should be inverted.  If the reflective surface follows an index specification of AIR, the stack is taken as defined (and should start with an index of 1.0).  If it follows glass, as in the case of a coating on a prism where the reflection is internal, the coating will be used intact only if defined as a stack where the incident medium is glass.  If the coating has air for the incident medium, and the reflection is internal, the program will reverse the order of layers – provided that the substrate extinction index K is zero.  This would be the case for a dielectric reflecting stack.  If the substrate K is not zero, the program will assume that the topmost layers of the stack are invisible, and will model only the (presumably metallic) substrate layer.  This allows use of the same overcoated metallic stacks for either air-air or glass-glass reflections, without having to specify which is which.
