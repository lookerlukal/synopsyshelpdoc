Numerous toolbar buttons are provided to help you manage your glass-table glasses.  Glass-table glasses currently in the lens (see GTB) are shown superimposed on the display as a green-colored circle, while glass model glasses (see GLM) are shown in red.

 

You can also reach the GlassMap options via the command MGT.

 

The GlassMap Dialog

 

 

Most of these toolbar buttons serve the same function as those in the SketchPAD display.  Most of the keyboard shortcuts found there also work in the GlassMap: <Shift>+Left-click to magnify, <Ctrl+Shift>+Left-click to minify, and drag with the right mouse button to pan.  You can also zoom in and out with the mouse wheel.

 

 

Dialog selections are:

 

Preferred

Shows only glasses designated as preferred by the manufacturer

 

All

Shows all glasses in the catalog

 

Spots Only

Glasses are shown only as colored dots; the colors indicate some of the glass types.

 

Numbers

The glass type number, but not the name, is shown.

 

Whole name

The full glass name is shown.  This is best viewed at magnified scale.

 

Apply

Assigns the glass type in the box labeled ‘Glass’ to the surface number in the box labeled ‘Surface’.  Click on a glass type to set the glass name, or on a lens vertex to enter that surface number (or just type the number).

 

Apply + Adjust

This assigns the selected glass to the selected surface and then performs a quick optimization to restore the power and bending of the element, using the IRG function.  This keeps the first-order properties of the lens relatively unchanged.  The Apply button, on the other hand, simply assigns the glass type without changing anything else, and can result in ray failures.  On the other hand, the optimization performed by this adjustment only controls first-order properties of the element, ignoring real rays – and that can itself result in ray failures. So there is no guaranteed way to change the glass type safely, and you are encouraged to try first the Apply, and if that produces ray failures, then try the Adjust version.

 

Properties

After selecting a glass by clicking in the glass map, you can click this button to display a summary of the properties of that glass.

 

Table

Click here to select a different glass table.

 

You can also jump to a different table by clicking one of the buttons on the right side of the display.

 

 

Locate

Prompts for the name of a glass, which will then be centered in the display.

 

Graph

Opens a dialog where you can select from a variety of glass properties to graph on the GlassMap.  These include chemical sensitivity, relative cost, etc.  You can also change the way the glass data are displayed.  See below for an example.  The items available in the Graph dialog are explained here.

 

Boundaries

This button displays the current boundaries, crown and flint, that will apply to glass variables of the form GLM, GBC, and GBF along with a small box with four slider bars that can be used to alter the slope and offset of the two lines.  The default boundaries outline the area in which commercial glasses are available, and these limits work well under most conditions – but you will sometimes wish to allow a glass to move along a particular path to see in which direction the lens wants to go.  In this case, you should adjust the line with the sliders to the path you want, and then reoptimize the lens, supplying limits on the index if it tends to go out of the region of interest.  The glass variables will then be constrained within (or on) that line.

 

Also displayed on the box is a list of the CBOUNDS and FBOUNDS input that may be put into the PANT file to alter the boundaries according to the settings you have selected.  It is not necessary to use these lines for optimization in the same session, since the current settings are honored automatically – but if you restart SYNOPSYS, the default settings come back, and then these lines will reset the boundaries to the values you had selected.

 

Transm. Range

Click here to open a dialog showing the transmission range of all of the materials in the current catalog.

 

Show List

This button displays a list of all glasses in the selected table.  You can sort various properties and get an overview of the whole table.  This is the same display produced by the SPS spreadsheet by a right-click on the GlassType link box.  If you click on a glass name and then OK, the selected glass is located for you on the displayed map.

 

Click here to put a bitmap copy of the glassmap on the Windows clipboard.  Then you can paste it into a graphics window for annotation or printing – or any third-party program that can accept graphics, such as Word or any graphics editor.

 

Clicking here changes the cursor to a circle; then click on a glass symbol to display the properties of that glass.  You can also display this dialog by clicking on a glass type and then clicking the “Properties” button.

 

To Graphics

This button formats the current glassmap display as a normal graphics picture that can be annotated, saved, or printed.

 

 

The Graph button opens a dialog where you can select the properties you want to examine – and also select the coordinates that will be displayed.  You can choose to show Nd vs. Vd, the index and V-number at the current lens wavelengths, a diagram of PFe vs. Ve, which is useful when you want to find glass combinations to make an apochromatic objective, and more.  For a discussion of how one uses the latter tool, go to http://www.osdoptics.com/APO_LESSON.htm.  There are other options too.  Click the Graph button, and then click Help in the dialog that opens to read about them.

 

 

This figure shows the absorption of some glasses at a wavelength of 0.35 uM.  The lengths of the vertical lines varies as the absorption; the red lines are preferred glasses, while the blue are not.  Glass type BALF8 shows no line – meaning that it absorbs very little at that wavelength, while TIF2 absorbs strongly.  (Both of those types are obsolete; the glass tables include those glasses to be compatible with legacy lens files.)

 

The data published by the glass vendors are not uniformly accurate.  You are encouraged to verify all critical results with the glass company.  Many glasses are not assigned all of the properties by the vendor.  In such cases, the property is given (with the Properties button) with a value of zero, and the graph shows a short horizontal line.

 

The transmission calculation employs a table of published values for most of the important materials, and an older polynomial approximation for the others.  If you want to verify that a particular glass uses the more accurate table, insert it into your lens and then use the command PGA SN, and the listing will say which it is.

 

When the unusual-materials catalog (U) is displayed, the program attempts to show the dispersions of the materials calculated for the wavelength range in effect in the current lens.  This helps you identify materials that may be useful in your design.  This cannot be done with materials whose transmission range does not include those wavelengths, however; in such cases the program instead calculates the Abbe number with the wavelengths over which the index function is defined, and not those in the current lens.

For lenses used in the visible spectrum, the rules are different.  (This applies to all catalogs except U and C.)  Here, if your lens contains a GTB glass and switch 78 is off, the location of the surface is marked on the map with a green symbol located at the glass itself.  If that switch is on, the location is computed from the effective N and V taken at the first three wavelengths in the color order.  This lets you see the effective properties of the glass at the design wavelengths of the lens, even if the latter are not the CDF lines.
