9.2 IFOV-Detector Efficiency (DETECTOR)        Next Previous TOC

 

Once the image model, or array of models, has been created as defined above, the DETECTOR program will combine it with a detector of specified dimensions, to determine the fraction of energy that will be received.  The input is

 

DETECTOR RECT XDS YDS XOS YOS

 

or

 

DETECTOR ROUND RADIUS XOS YOS

 

A related feature that will find the radius that encloses a given fraction of the energy is described in the next section.

 

The shape, size, and position of the detector are given by the DET command, where XDS and YDS are the X and Y dimensions of the rectangular detector, and RADIUS is the radius of the round detector.  The center of the detector is located at XOS. YOS in the same coordinate system as the image model (see above).

 

For AFOCAL systems, the MODELs and detector dimensions are in angular coordinates (radians).

 

As each DET line is read, the composite image is superimposed on the detector and the fraction of the total energy falling thereon is computed and displayed.  You may input as many DET lines as desired, and each will use the most recent set of models.

 

You may obtain a listing of each image model by turning on switch 15 prior to the input.  Each model consists of an array of samples and associated intensities.  For GMODELs, each ray is a sample; for DMODELs the diffraction pattern is broken up into an array of partial images, diagrammed above.  The X, Y coordinates of each sample are printed along with the relative intensity of that sample.  An elementary use of this feature is to generate a listing of each ray in a spot diagram. (The SPT command will also produce such a listing, with switch 50 on.)

 

For accurate results using the diffraction model DMODEL, the detector size should have one dimension larger than 1/10 the radius of the diffraction pattern.  If this is not the case, an informative warning message will be printed.

 

To normalize the image analysis, most features take the total of the sampled energy as 100%.  The exception is for DMODELs with a circular detector.  For this case, which is known from theory, the program estimates the (small) fraction of unsampled energy (because the sample pattern does not extend to infinity) and includes this as well.

 

 

Examples of DET input

 

1. Assume we have a square detector of .010 inches on a side, and an optical system entered in an RLE file.  We wish to know the amount of energy that would otherwise fall on the detector, but which spills over due to diffraction.

 

DMODEL 2 0 200 1                (We use the on-axis image as a model.)

OV5

IFOV REC .01 .01

DET REC .01 .01

 

2. The optics makes a long, thin off-axis image, oriented radially away from the axis.  We wish to get the waveform as the image sweeps past a slightly non-radial slit.  Because of lateral color, the waveform depends on the color of the object.  Assume that the RLE file contained

 

OBB 0 2 3 0 2

 

specifying an X- and Y-object field of view of 2 degrees.  The image is to be combined with a slit of .001 inches width parallel to the Y-axis, but separated from it by .025 inches, which corresponds to a GBAR object coordinate of 0.012.

 

 

MODEL M 1 200 1 .012

OV5

DET REC .001 10 -.005

DET REC .001 10 -.004

DET REC .001 10 -.003

DET REC .001 10 -.002

etc.

 

The above data will store three image models, in colors 2, 1, and 3, and then combine them with a slit of length 10 and width .001 positioned at successive X-coordinates of -.005, -.004, -.003, etc. with respect to the chief ray coordinate in the primary color, color 2.  (A simpler way to obtain this analysis will be given in the next section.)
